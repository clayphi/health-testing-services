## Inflammation and Immune Markers

### 1. hs-CRP (High-Sensitivity C-Reactive Protein)

| Field | Content |
|-------|---------|
| **What it measures** | C-reactive protein produced by the liver in response to inflammation. The "high-sensitivity" assay detects very low concentrations (0.3-10 mg/L), making it suitable for cardiovascular risk stratification rather than just acute infection detection. |
| **Why it matters** | Independent predictor of cardiovascular events, including heart attack and stroke. Elevated hs-CRP reflects systemic vascular inflammation ("angry arteries") that contributes to atherosclerotic plaque development and instability. Also correlated with cancer risk, metabolic syndrome, and all-cause mortality. |
| **Standard reference range** | Low risk: <1.0 mg/L; Average risk: 1.0-3.0 mg/L; High risk: >3.0 mg/L (AHA/CDC classification). Values >10 mg/L suggest acute infection/illness, not chronic inflammation -- retest in 2 weeks. |
| **Optimal range** | <0.55 mg/L for men (Life Extension recommendation). Many longevity practitioners target <0.5 mg/L. Peter Attia considers hs-CRP a useful adjunct but not a standalone marker -- prefers Lp-PLA2 for vascular-specific inflammation. |
| **High values indicate** | Chronic systemic inflammation, cardiovascular disease risk, metabolic syndrome, obesity, autoimmune conditions, chronic infection, periodontal disease, sleep apnea, cancer risk. Values >10 mg/L indicate acute infection, injury, or illness. |
| **Low values indicate** | Low systemic inflammation. No known clinical concerns with very low values. Generally reflects good metabolic health, healthy body composition, and absence of chronic inflammatory conditions. |
| **Special considerations** | **Exercise within 24-48 hours can significantly elevate hs-CRP** -- intense exercise triggers an acute-phase inflammatory response. Also elevated by acute illness, injury, infection, surgery, and dental procedures -- these are NOT reflective of chronic cardiovascular inflammation. Multiple measurements (at least 2, taken 2 weeks apart) are recommended for accurate risk assessment. Statins, aspirin, omega-3s, and weight loss all lower hs-CRP. Oral contraceptives and HRT elevate it. Obesity strongly correlates with elevated hs-CRP due to IL-6 release from adipose tissue. |
| **Interactions with other markers** | Interpret alongside **Lp-PLA2** (vascular-specific inflammation), **fibrinogen** (another acute-phase reactant), **ApoB/LDL-P** (atherogenic particle burden), **fasting insulin** (metabolic inflammation), **IL-6** (upstream cytokine). When hs-CRP is elevated but Lp-PLA2 is normal, inflammation may be non-vascular in origin. |

### 2. Homocysteine

| Field | Content |
|-------|---------|
| **What it measures** | An amino acid produced during methionine metabolism. Requires B12, folate (B9), and B6 for proper recycling back to methionine or conversion to cysteine. Elevated levels indicate impaired methylation. |
| **Why it matters** | Independent cardiovascular risk factor. Each 5 umol/L increase raises coronary heart disease risk by approximately 20%. Also associated with cognitive decline, dementia, Alzheimer's disease, stroke, peripheral artery disease, blood clots, depression, and pregnancy complications. Reflects methylation cycle function, which affects DNA repair, detoxification, and neurotransmitter synthesis. |
| **Standard reference range** | Normal: 5-15 umol/L. Moderately elevated: 15-30 umol/L. Intermediate: 30-100 umol/L. Severe: >100 umol/L. |
| **Optimal range** | <7 umol/L (functional medicine consensus). Ideal is <6 umol/L for lowest cardiovascular and cognitive risk. Life Extension targets <8 umol/L as ideal, <12 umol/L as acceptable. Risk rises continuously from approximately 6-7 umol/L -- a level of 12 umol/L, while "normal" by conventional standards, represents meaningfully elevated risk. |
| **High values indicate** | B12 deficiency, folate deficiency, B6 deficiency, **MTHFR gene variants** (C677T, A1298C -- impair folate metabolism), chronic kidney disease, hypothyroidism, excessive methionine intake, certain medications (methotrexate, phenytoin, carbamazepine, nitrous oxide), excessive alcohol use, smoking, aging. |
| **Low values indicate** | Generally not clinically concerning. May reflect excellent B-vitamin status and methylation function. Very low levels (<4 umol/L) are uncommon and not typically associated with adverse outcomes. |
| **Special considerations** | **Must be interpreted alongside B12 and folate levels.** Elevated homocysteine with low B12 points to B12 deficiency. Elevated with low folate points to folate deficiency. If both B12 and folate are adequate, consider **MTHFR genetic testing**. Patients with MTHFR variants should use methylfolate (5-MTHF) rather than folic acid, and methylcobalamin rather than cyanocobalamin. Treatment: methylcobalamin 1,000-5,000 mcg/day + methylfolate 800-5,000 mcg/day + P5P (active B6) 25-50 mg/day. TMG (trimethylglycine) is an additional option. Homocysteine typically normalizes within 4-8 weeks of B-vitamin repletion. Fasting is recommended but not strictly required. |
| **Interactions with other markers** | Interpret alongside **vitamin B12**, **folate/5-MTHF**, **vitamin B6**, **methylmalonic acid** (MMA -- more specific for B12 deficiency), **hs-CRP** (both reflect cardiovascular risk), **MTHFR status** if available. Kidney function (creatinine/cystatin C) should also be assessed, as impaired renal clearance raises homocysteine. |

### 3. ESR (Erythrocyte Sedimentation Rate)

| Field | Content |
|-------|---------|
| **What it measures** | The rate at which red blood cells settle to the bottom of a standardized tube over one hour (mm/hr). When inflammation is present, red blood cells clump together (due to increased fibrinogen and immunoglobulins), become heavier, and settle faster. |
| **Why it matters** | Non-specific marker of systemic inflammation. Useful for detecting and monitoring inflammatory conditions such as autoimmune diseases (rheumatoid arthritis, lupus, polymyalgia rheumatica), infections, and malignancies. Less specific than hs-CRP for cardiovascular risk but provides complementary information. |
| **Standard reference range** | Men under 50: 0-15 mm/hr. Men over 50: 0-20 mm/hr. A commonly used formula: upper limit = age / 2 for males. |
| **Optimal range** | 0-10 mm/hr for an active adult. Lower is generally better. Active, fit individuals typically have values in the low single digits. |
| **High values indicate** | Infection (bacterial, viral, fungal), autoimmune disease (RA, lupus, vasculitis, IBD), malignancy (lymphoma, multiple myeloma), tissue necrosis, chronic kidney disease, anemia, obesity, pregnancy, aging. Values >100 mm/hr suggest serious active disease (malignancy, severe infection, vasculitis). |
| **Low values indicate** | Polycythemia vera, sickle cell disease, extreme leukocytosis, congestive heart failure, hypofibrinogenemia, very low plasma protein levels. In the absence of these conditions, low ESR is normal and desirable. |
| **Special considerations** | Very non-specific -- elevated by many conditions. Changes slowly (days to weeks) compared to CRP (hours). Affected by anemia (raises ESR falsely), polycythemia (lowers ESR), and macrocytosis. NSAIDs and aspirin can lower ESR. Not affected by exercise as acutely as hs-CRP. Best used as a screening tool alongside more specific markers. Age naturally increases ESR. |
| **Interactions with other markers** | Interpret alongside **hs-CRP** (faster-responding inflammation marker), **CBC** (to rule out anemia or polycythemia affecting results), **fibrinogen** (acute-phase protein that drives ESR elevation), **ferritin** (another acute-phase reactant), **protein electrophoresis** (if markedly elevated, to rule out myeloma). |

### 4. Fibrinogen

| Field | Content |
|-------|---------|
| **What it measures** | A glycoprotein produced by the liver that is converted to fibrin during blood clot formation. It is both a coagulation factor (Factor I) and an acute-phase reactant that rises with inflammation. |
| **Why it matters** | Dual role as both a clotting factor and inflammatory marker. Elevated fibrinogen independently predicts cardiovascular events, stroke, and peripheral vascular disease. Promotes platelet aggregation, increases blood viscosity, and contributes to thrombus formation. High levels are associated with atherosclerotic plaque progression. |
| **Standard reference range** | 200-400 mg/dL (2.0-4.0 g/L). Critical low value: <100 mg/dL (bleeding risk). |
| **Optimal range** | 200-300 mg/dL. Longevity practitioners prefer the lower half of the standard range. Levels >350 mg/dL, even within "normal," indicate elevated cardiovascular and thrombotic risk. The best cut-off for predicting severity of coronary stenosis is approximately 347.5 mg/dL (3.475 g/L). |
| **High values indicate** | Acute inflammation/infection, cardiovascular disease risk, stroke risk, smoking, obesity, metabolic syndrome, diabetes, pregnancy, oral contraceptive use, chronic kidney disease, malignancy, post-surgical state, tissue injury. Elevated fibrinogen increases blood viscosity and clot risk. |
| **Low values indicate** | Liver disease/failure (decreased production), DIC (disseminated intravascular coagulation -- consumption of fibrinogen), severe malnutrition, fibrinolytic therapy, congenital hypofibrinogenemia or afibrinogenemia. <100 mg/dL is clinically critical. |
| **Special considerations** | Acute-phase reactant -- rises within 24-48 hours of tissue injury, surgery, or infection. Smoking significantly elevates fibrinogen. Regular exercise lowers it. Omega-3 fatty acids, niacin, and fibrates can reduce fibrinogen. Statin effect on fibrinogen is modest. Blood draws should ideally be fasting, though not strictly required. Oral contraceptives and HRT can raise levels. |
| **Interactions with other markers** | Interpret alongside **hs-CRP** (both are acute-phase reactants -- concordant elevation suggests systemic inflammation), **ESR** (fibrinogen is the primary driver of ESR elevation), **D-dimer** (if fibrinogen is low, assess for DIC), **platelet count**, **PT/INR** (coagulation cascade), **ApoB/lipid panel** (cardiovascular risk), **ferritin** (another acute-phase reactant). |

## Advanced Cardiovascular Markers

### 5. ApoB (Apolipoprotein B)

| Field | Content |
|-------|---------|
| **What it measures** | Apolipoprotein B-100 is the single structural protein found on every atherogenic lipoprotein particle: LDL, VLDL, IDL, and Lp(a). One ApoB molecule per particle, so ApoB directly counts the total number of atherogenic particles in circulation. |
| **Why it matters** | **Peter Attia considers ApoB the single most important cardiovascular marker.** It is superior to LDL-C because it counts ALL atherogenic particles rather than estimating cholesterol mass within them. When LDL-C and ApoB disagree ("discordance"), cardiovascular risk tracks with ApoB, not LDL-C. This is especially important in metabolic syndrome, insulin resistance, and hypertriglyceridemia where LDL-C underestimates true particle burden. Cumulative lifetime exposure to atherogenic particles drives atherosclerosis -- reducing ApoB earlier provides compounding benefit. Each 10 mg/dL reduction in ApoB drops heart disease risk by approximately 9%. |
| **Standard reference range** | Normal: <100 mg/dL. Borderline high: 100-129 mg/dL. High: >130 mg/dL. NLA high-risk target: <90 mg/dL. NLA highest-risk target: <80 mg/dL. |
| **Optimal range** | **<80 mg/dL** (20th population percentile -- achievable for most with inexpensive statin therapy). **<60 mg/dL** for aggressive prevention (Peter Attia's target -- 5th population percentile, may require statin + PCSK9 inhibitor). Attia notes that physiologic levels in children and hunter-gatherer populations are 10-30 mg/dL with no negative consequences. The lower the better -- there is no known lower threshold below which ApoB becomes harmful. |
| **High values indicate** | Elevated atherogenic particle burden, increased cardiovascular risk, familial hypercholesterolemia, metabolic syndrome, insulin resistance, type 2 diabetes, hypothyroidism, nephrotic syndrome, obesity. High ApoB with "normal" LDL-C is a particularly dangerous discordance pattern. |
| **Low values indicate** | Low atherogenic particle burden (desirable). Very low levels (<40 mg/dL) may occur with hypobetalipoproteinemia (genetic) or aggressive lipid-lowering therapy. No adverse health effects from low ApoB levels have been demonstrated. Abetalipoproteinemia (absent ApoB) is a rare genetic condition with fat malabsorption. |
| **Special considerations** | Fasting is NOT required (unlike triglycerides and calculated LDL-C). ApoB is stable post-meal. ApoB should be the primary target for lipid-lowering therapy, not LDL-C. Statins reduce ApoB 35-55%. Ezetimibe adds 15-25%. PCSK9 inhibitors add further reduction. Diet, exercise, and weight loss provide modest reductions. ApoB is a better predictor of on-treatment residual risk than LDL-C. |
| **Interactions with other markers** | Interpret alongside **LDL-C** (discordance analysis -- if ApoB high but LDL-C normal, risk is high), **triglycerides** (high TG drives small dense LDL particles, increasing ApoB relative to LDL-C), **LDL-P** (NMR-based particle count -- should correlate with ApoB), **Lp(a)** (contributes to ApoB count), **hs-CRP/Lp-PLA2** (inflammation accelerates ApoB-driven atherosclerosis), **insulin/HOMA-IR** (insulin resistance drives discordance). |

### 6. Lp(a) (Lipoprotein(a))

| Field | Content |
|-------|---------|
| **What it measures** | A lipoprotein particle consisting of an LDL-like core bound to apolipoprotein(a). Structurally similar to LDL but with an additional apo(a) protein that shares homology with plasminogen (a clot-dissolving protein), giving Lp(a) both atherogenic AND thrombotic properties. |
| **Why it matters** | Independent, causal risk factor for atherosclerotic cardiovascular disease, aortic valve stenosis, and stroke. **More than 90% genetically determined by the LPA gene** -- one of the strongest genetic risk factors for coronary artery disease. Each Lp(a) particle is estimated to be 6.6x more atherogenic per-particle than ordinary LDL. Lp(a) promotes atherogenesis, inflammation, and thrombosis. Remains a risk factor even when LDL-C is well controlled. |
| **Standard reference range** | Normal: <30 mg/dL (<75 nmol/L). Intermediate/"gray zone": 30-50 mg/dL (75-125 nmol/L). High/elevated: >50 mg/dL (>125 nmol/L). Risk increases linearly above the median, with the steepest risk in the top 5-10% of the population. |
| **Optimal range** | <14 mg/dL (<30 nmol/L) is ideal. <30 mg/dL (<75 nmol/L) is desirable per EAS/NLA. **However, Lp(a) levels are genetically fixed and cannot be meaningfully changed with lifestyle.** If elevated, the clinical response is to aggressively reduce all OTHER modifiable risk factors (ApoB, blood pressure, metabolic health, inflammation). |
| **High values indicate** | Genetically elevated Lp(a) -- increased risk of ASCVD, myocardial infarction, stroke, aortic valve calcification/stenosis, peripheral arterial disease, and heart failure. Risk is continuous and multiplicative with other risk factors. Elevated Lp(a) is the most common genetic dyslipidemia, affecting 20-25% of the global population. |
| **Low values indicate** | Lower genetically-determined cardiovascular risk from this pathway. Low Lp(a) is not associated with any adverse health effects. |
| **Special considerations** | **Only needs to be tested once in a lifetime** -- levels are >90% genetically determined and do not change meaningfully with diet, exercise, or most medications. Statins slightly INCREASE Lp(a) (10-20%). Niacin reduces Lp(a) modestly (20-30%) but has not shown outcomes benefit. PCSK9 inhibitors reduce Lp(a) by approximately 20-30%. **No FDA-approved Lp(a)-lowering therapy exists yet**, but clinical trials are ongoing: pelacarsen (ASO), olpasiran and zerlasiran (siRNAs), and lepodisiran (siRNA) are in Phase 2-3 trials with results expected 2025-2027. mg/dL and nmol/L are NOT directly convertible (the ratio varies by apo(a) isoform size) -- labs should report in one consistent unit. The AHA issued updated guidelines in March 2026 recommending Lp(a) testing at least once for all adults. |
| **Interactions with other markers** | Interpret alongside **ApoB** (Lp(a) carries one ApoB molecule, so contributes to total ApoB count -- but ApoB-lowering therapy does not address Lp(a)-specific risk), **LDL-C** (standard LDL-C assays include Lp(a) cholesterol, so true "LDL-C" is lower than reported when Lp(a) is high), **hs-CRP/Lp-PLA2** (inflammation amplifies Lp(a) risk), **fibrinogen/D-dimer** (Lp(a) has prothrombotic properties via plasminogen homology), **coronary artery calcium score** (CAC provides anatomic confirmation of Lp(a)-driven atherosclerosis). |

### 7. LDL Particle Number (LDL-P) / NMR LipoProfile

| Field | Content |
|-------|---------|
| **What it measures** | The total number of LDL particles in circulation, measured by nuclear magnetic resonance (NMR) spectroscopy. Unlike LDL-C (which measures cholesterol mass within LDL), LDL-P directly counts particles. The NMR LipoProfile also reports small LDL-P, LDL size, HDL-P, and VLDL size. |
| **Why it matters** | LDL-P is a stronger predictor of cardiovascular events than LDL-C in multiple large studies (Framingham, MESA). When LDL-C and LDL-P are discordant, cardiovascular risk tracks with particle number, not cholesterol mass. This is because each LDL particle can penetrate the arterial wall regardless of how much cholesterol it carries. Small, dense LDL particles carry less cholesterol per particle, so LDL-C underestimates risk when particles are small and numerous. |
| **Standard reference range** | Low risk: <1,000 nmol/L (20th percentile). Moderate: 1,000-1,299 nmol/L. Borderline high: 1,300-1,599 nmol/L. High: 1,600-2,000 nmol/L. Very high: >2,000 nmol/L. Reference population: MESA study (5,362 men and women not on lipid medication). |
| **Optimal range** | <1,000 nmol/L (20th percentile). For aggressive cardiovascular prevention: <700-800 nmol/L. High-risk patients on therapy achieving mean LDL-P of 860 nmol/L had significantly fewer cardiovascular events than those achieving low LDL-C alone. Longevity practitioners generally target the lowest achievable LDL-P. |
| **High values indicate** | Elevated atherogenic particle burden, increased cardiovascular risk. Especially important when LDL-C appears "normal" but LDL-P is elevated (discordance) -- common in metabolic syndrome, insulin resistance, type 2 diabetes, and hypertriglyceridemia. High proportion of small dense LDL particles. |
| **Low values indicate** | Low atherogenic particle burden (desirable). Low LDL-P with high LDL-C (discordant low) suggests large, buoyant LDL particles -- associated with lower risk than LDL-C alone would suggest. |
| **Special considerations** | NMR LipoProfile is not universally available -- primarily offered through Labcorp (LipoScience) and Cleveland HeartLab. ApoB is more widely available, less expensive, and correlates closely with LDL-P. Many experts (including Peter Attia) now prefer ApoB over LDL-P due to wider availability and standardization, but LDL-P provides additional information on particle size distribution. Fasting is technically preferred for accurate LDL-C component, but LDL-P itself is not significantly affected by fasting status. Statins reduce LDL-P by 35-55%. |
| **Interactions with other markers** | Interpret alongside **ApoB** (should correlate closely -- one ApoB per LDL particle), **LDL-C** (discordance analysis is the primary value), **triglycerides** (high TG drives small dense LDL, increasing LDL-P relative to LDL-C), **small LDL-P** (high proportion of small particles indicates insulin resistance), **HDL-P** (protective particle count), **insulin/HOMA-IR** (insulin resistance is the primary driver of LDL-C/LDL-P discordance). |

### 8. LP-PLA2 (Lipoprotein-Associated Phospholipase A2)

| Field | Content |
|-------|---------|
| **What it measures** | An enzyme produced by monocytes and macrophages that breaks down oxidized phospholipids within arterial plaque. Also known as the PLAC test. Unlike hs-CRP (systemic inflammation), LP-PLA2 specifically reflects vascular wall inflammation and active, unstable atherosclerotic plaque. |
| **Why it matters** | Marker of "angry arteries" -- specifically indicates active, growing, soft (vulnerable) plaque. Predicts cardiovascular events independent of LDL-C and other inflammatory markers. Associated with 2-fold increased risk of coronary events and 2-fold increased risk of stroke. Peter Attia has stated he considers LP-PLA2 superior to hs-CRP as a marker of vascular disease. |
| **Standard reference range** | Optimal: <180 nmol/min/mL. Borderline: 180-224 nmol/min/mL. Increased risk: >225 nmol/min/mL. (Note: some labs report mass in ng/mL rather than activity in nmol/min/mL -- ensure you know which assay your lab uses.) |
| **Optimal range** | <180 nmol/min/mL (activity). Lower is better. Longevity practitioners target well below the borderline threshold. Elevated LP-PLA2 in the context of low hs-CRP is especially concerning as it suggests isolated vascular inflammation. |
| **High values indicate** | Active vascular inflammation, unstable/vulnerable atherosclerotic plaque, increased risk of coronary events and stroke, active plaque progression. Elevated LP-PLA2 is particularly concerning when combined with elevated ApoB/LDL-P (high particle burden + vascular inflammation). |
| **Low values indicate** | Low vascular inflammation, stable or minimal atherosclerotic plaque activity. Desirable result. |
| **Special considerations** | Not affected by acute infections or exercise the way hs-CRP is -- making it a more specific marker of vascular inflammation. Statins reduce LP-PLA2 significantly. Niacin also reduces LP-PLA2. Darapladib (a direct LP-PLA2 inhibitor) was tested in clinical trials but failed to reduce cardiovascular events, suggesting LP-PLA2 is a marker of disease activity rather than a direct therapeutic target. Fasting is preferred. EDTA plasma or serum depending on the lab. |
| **Interactions with other markers** | Interpret alongside **hs-CRP** (if LP-PLA2 high + hs-CRP high: systemic + vascular inflammation; if LP-PLA2 high + hs-CRP low: isolated vascular disease), **ApoB/LDL-P** (atherogenic particle burden), **oxidized LDL** (substrate for LP-PLA2 activity), **Lp(a)** (additional atherogenic risk), **coronary artery calcium score** (anatomic correlate). LP-PLA2 and hs-CRP together provide better risk stratification than either alone. |

### 9. Oxidized LDL

| Field | Content |
|-------|---------|
| **What it measures** | LDL cholesterol particles that have been chemically modified by oxidation (free radical damage). Oxidized LDL (oxLDL) is the form of LDL that is directly taken up by macrophages in arterial walls to form foam cells -- the hallmark of early atherosclerotic plaque formation. |
| **Why it matters** | Native (non-oxidized) LDL is relatively benign; it is the oxidation of LDL that initiates and accelerates atherosclerosis. OxLDL triggers endothelial dysfunction, promotes monocyte recruitment, drives foam cell formation, and stimulates inflammatory cytokine release. Elevated oxLDL is a sensitive marker of coronary artery disease and predicts cardiovascular events. A 3.5-fold increased relative risk is seen with levels >70 U/L. |
| **Standard reference range** | Quest Diagnostics: <60 U/L. Labcorp: 10-170 ng/mL. (Note: units differ by lab -- Quest uses U/L, Labcorp uses ng/mL. Confirm which assay your lab uses.) |
| **Optimal range** | <60 U/L (Quest assay). The ODX (functional/optimal) range: 0-37 U/L. For Labcorp: 0-108 ng/mL optimal. Lower is better -- reflects lower oxidative stress and less atherogenic modification of LDL particles. |
| **High values indicate** | Increased oxidative stress, active atherosclerotic plaque formation, elevated cardiovascular risk, metabolic syndrome, diabetes, smoking exposure, chronic inflammation, obesity, poor antioxidant status, environmental toxin exposure. |
| **Low values indicate** | Low oxidative modification of LDL particles. Good antioxidant defense. Lower atherogenic drive. Desirable result. |
| **Special considerations** | Not yet part of standard cardiovascular panels -- must be specifically ordered. Antioxidant-rich diets (polyphenols, vitamin E, vitamin C) reduce oxLDL. Smoking significantly increases oxLDL. Exercise reduces oxLDL over time but acute intense exercise may transiently increase oxidative stress. Omega-3 fatty acids and Mediterranean diet reduce oxLDL. Statins reduce oxLDL by reducing LDL particle substrate. Fasting is preferred. The oxLDL/LDL-C ratio may be more informative than absolute oxLDL alone. |
| **Interactions with other markers** | Interpret alongside **LDL-C and ApoB** (more LDL particles = more substrate for oxidation), **LP-PLA2** (acts on oxidized phospholipids within plaque), **hs-CRP** (oxidative stress drives inflammation), **Omega-3 Index** (anti-inflammatory, reduces oxidation), **vitamin D** (antioxidant defense), **fasting glucose/insulin** (metabolic dysfunction promotes oxidation), **uric acid** (can act as both pro- and anti-oxidant). |

### 10. Omega-3 Index

| Field | Content |
|-------|---------|
| **What it measures** | The percentage of EPA (eicosapentaenoic acid) + DHA (docosahexaenoic acid) in red blood cell membranes, expressed as a percentage of total fatty acids. Reflects long-term (approximately 120-day) omega-3 tissue status, not just recent dietary intake. |
| **Why it matters** | **>8% is optimal, <4% is high risk. Most Americans are deficient (average 4-5%).** An Omega-3 Index above 8% is associated with reduced risk of fatal coronary heart disease (35% lower), sudden cardiac death (81% lower risk above 4.98% vs below 3.45% in the Physicians' Health Study), cognitive decline, depression, all-cause mortality, inflammation, non-alcoholic fatty liver disease, and premature birth. Omega-3 fatty acids are anti-inflammatory, anti-arrhythmic, and reduce platelet aggregation. |
| **Standard reference range** | 2.5-16%. Risk zones: <4% = high risk; 4-8% = intermediate risk; >8% = desirable/low risk. |
| **Optimal range** | **8-12%**. Target >8% as the minimum for cardiovascular protection. 8-12% is the range observed in populations with low cardiovascular disease rates (Japan, Mediterranean countries). Over 12% is not known to be harmful but does not appear to confer additional benefit. Dose-response research suggests increasing EPA+DHA intake by 1.5 grams/day raises the index by approximately 4 percentage points. |
| **High values indicate** | Excellent omega-3 tissue status and dietary intake. Lower cardiovascular risk, better inflammatory balance, better cognitive health. Values >16% are rare and reflect very high supplementation. |
| **Low values indicate** | Omega-3 deficiency, increased cardiovascular risk, higher inflammatory state, increased risk of sudden cardiac death, cognitive decline, depression, compromised red blood cell membrane integrity. Common in Western diets, vegans, athletes, and young people. |
| **Special considerations** | Fasting is NOT required. Based on red blood cell membrane composition, which reflects intake over the prior 3-4 months -- a single meal will not affect results. Retest 3-4 months after initiating supplementation to allow full RBC turnover. Once optimal (8-12%), annual monitoring is sufficient. Athletes and vegans tend to have lower baseline values. Fish oil supplements (2-4 g/day of combined EPA+DHA) are typically needed to reach 8%+ for people who don't eat fatty fish 3+ times per week. AA/EPA ratio is a complementary marker of inflammatory balance (optimal <3:1). |
| **Interactions with other markers** | Interpret alongside **hs-CRP** (omega-3s are anti-inflammatory -- low O3I correlates with higher hs-CRP), **triglycerides** (omega-3s lower TG significantly), **oxidized LDL** (omega-3s reduce oxidative modification), **AA/EPA ratio** (omega-6:omega-3 balance), **LP-PLA2** (vascular inflammation), **fibrinogen** (omega-3s reduce fibrinogen), **red blood cell distribution width (RDW)** (low O3I associated with increased RDW). |

## Other Markers

### 11. IGF-1 (Insulin-like Growth Factor 1)

| Field | Content |
|-------|---------|
| **What it measures** | A peptide hormone primarily produced by the liver in response to growth hormone (GH) stimulation. IGF-1 mediates most of GH's anabolic effects: muscle growth, bone density, tissue repair, and cell proliferation. Serves as the primary clinical proxy for growth hormone status since GH itself is pulsatile and difficult to measure reliably. |
| **Why it matters** | **There is a U-shaped curve for IGF-1 and mortality.** Too high may increase cancer risk (promotes cell proliferation in existing tumors); too low is associated with frailty, sarcopenia, poor recovery, cardiovascular disease, and increased mortality. The sweet spot balances tissue repair and muscle maintenance against cancer risk. A 2019 meta-analysis of 30,000+ participants confirmed that IGF-1 levels in the middle range (120-160 ng/mL) were associated with the lowest all-cause mortality. Both very low and very high levels increased mortality risk. |
| **Standard reference range** | Age-dependent, declines with age. For an active adult male: approximately 84-270 ng/mL (varies by assay; iSYS assay: 91-233 ng/mL for ages 40-49; UI Health Care: 92-221 ng/mL at age 43). |
| **Optimal range** | **120-180 ng/mL** for an active adult male focused on longevity. This mid-range balances anabolic benefits (muscle maintenance, bone density, tissue repair, cognitive function) against cancer-promoting risk. Some longevity practitioners target 120-160 ng/mL specifically based on the meta-analysis data showing lowest mortality in this range. Avoid both extremes. |
| **High values indicate** | Acromegaly (GH-secreting pituitary adenoma), exogenous GH or IGF-1 use, high dairy/animal protein intake, insulin resistance (hyperinsulinemia stimulates IGF-1), obesity, possibly increased cancer risk (prostate, breast, colorectal, thyroid). UK Biobank data links elevated IGF-1 to higher risk of several cancers. |
| **Low values indicate** | Growth hormone deficiency, pituitary dysfunction, liver disease (liver produces IGF-1), malnutrition, anorexia, chronic illness, hypothyroidism, aging (natural decline), increased frailty risk, sarcopenia, poor wound healing, reduced bone density, cognitive decline, increased cardiovascular mortality. Very low IGF-1 is even more concerning in those with liver disease. |
| **Special considerations** | Influenced by diet: high dairy, animal protein, and carbohydrate intake raise IGF-1. Caloric restriction and fasting lower IGF-1. Protein intake is the strongest dietary driver. Exercise (especially resistance training) raises IGF-1 acutely. Sleep quality affects GH secretion and therefore IGF-1. Fasting is recommended for the blood draw. Time of day is less critical than for cortisol since IGF-1 does not follow a strong diurnal rhythm. Recheck every 6-12 months if managing levels. |
| **Interactions with other markers** | Interpret alongside **IGFBP-3** (binding protein; IGF-1/IGFBP-3 ratio reflects bioavailable IGF-1), **growth hormone** (if IGF-1 is low, GH stimulation testing may be needed), **fasting insulin/HOMA-IR** (hyperinsulinemia stimulates IGF-1 production), **testosterone** (anabolic axis -- both affect muscle/bone), **albumin** (nutritional status affecting IGF-1 production), **liver function tests** (liver disease reduces IGF-1). |

### 12. Cystatin C

| Field | Content |
|-------|---------|
| **What it measures** | A small protein produced at a constant rate by all nucleated cells, freely filtered by the kidneys, and almost entirely reabsorbed and catabolized in the proximal tubule. Used to estimate glomerular filtration rate (eGFR) as an alternative to creatinine. |
| **Why it matters** | **More accurate kidney function assessment than creatinine, especially for muscular/athletic people.** Creatinine is generated from muscle metabolism, so people with high muscle mass (like a active male) will have falsely elevated creatinine that underestimates their true kidney function (eGFR). Cystatin C is NOT affected by muscle mass, age, sex, race, diet, or creatine supplements. The combined creatinine-cystatin C equation achieves 84-94% accuracy vs gold-standard GFR, compared to 80-84% for creatinine alone. A NEJM study of 90,000+ participants found 13.7% had CKD by cystatin C testing vs only 9.7% by creatinine -- a 41% increase in disease detection. |
| **Standard reference range** | 0.55-1.15 mg/L (varies slightly by assay). Values above 1.0-1.15 mg/L suggest reduced kidney function. Clinical decisions should rely on eGFR derived from validated equations (CKD-EPI 2021 cystatin C or combined creatinine-cystatin C equation) rather than raw cystatin C values. |
| **Optimal range** | 0.6-0.85 mg/L. Corresponds to an eGFR >90 mL/min/1.73m2 (normal kidney function). For a young, healthy, active male, values in the lower half of the reference range are expected. Cystatin C-based eGFR >90 is the target. |
| **High values indicate** | Reduced glomerular filtration rate / kidney dysfunction. Chronic kidney disease (stages correlated with degree of elevation). Acute kidney injury. Cardiovascular disease risk (cystatin C independently predicts CV events beyond eGFR). Diabetes-related nephropathy. Hypertensive nephrosclerosis. Note: may be slightly elevated by high-dose corticosteroid therapy, hyperthyroidism, and active inflammation/obesity. |
| **Low values indicate** | Generally reflects excellent kidney function. Very low values are not clinically concerning. Hypothyroidism may lower cystatin C. |
| **Special considerations** | **Critically important for this patient profile.** A active male likely has elevated creatinine from muscle mass alone -- creatinine-based eGFR will underestimate true kidney function. Cystatin C eliminates this bias. Also essential for patients taking creatine supplements (common in active/athletic individuals), which falsely elevate creatinine. Cystatin C rises earlier than creatinine in acute kidney injury (peaks at 8 hours vs 48 hours). Not affected by dietary meat intake. May be slightly influenced by inflammation, obesity, smoking, and thyroid dysfunction. The 2021 CKD-EPI combined creatinine-cystatin C equation is the gold standard for eGFR estimation. |
| **Interactions with other markers** | Interpret alongside **creatinine + eGFR** (if creatinine-based eGFR is lower than cystatin C-based eGFR, muscle mass is the likely explanation), **BUN** (kidney function), **urine albumin-to-creatinine ratio (UACR)** (kidney damage vs function), **fasting glucose/HbA1c** (diabetic nephropathy screening), **blood pressure** (hypertensive kidney disease), **uric acid** (elevates with declining GFR). Peter Attia has discussed cystatin C as the preferred kidney function marker for fit/muscular patients. |

### 13. Cortisol (Morning, Fasting)

| Field | Content |
|-------|---------|
| **What it measures** | The primary glucocorticoid ("stress hormone") produced by the adrenal cortex. Regulates metabolism, immune function, blood pressure, blood sugar, and the stress response. Follows a strong diurnal (circadian) rhythm: highest upon waking (6-8 AM), declining throughout the day, lowest around midnight. |
| **Why it matters** | Cortisol dysregulation is associated with chronic stress, HPA axis dysfunction, adrenal insufficiency (Addison's disease), Cushing's syndrome, metabolic dysfunction, immune suppression, poor sleep, cognitive decline, and muscle wasting. Chronically elevated cortisol promotes visceral fat accumulation, insulin resistance, bone loss, and immune suppression. Chronically low cortisol (adrenal insufficiency) causes fatigue, hypotension, and inability to mount a stress response. |
| **Standard reference range** | Morning (6-8 AM, fasting): 10-20 mcg/dL (275-555 nmol/L). Late afternoon (around 4 PM): 3-10 mcg/dL. Normal function indicated by morning level >10 mcg/dL. Adrenal insufficiency suspected if morning level <5 mcg/dL. |
| **Optimal range** | 10-15 mcg/dL (morning, fasting, 6-8 AM draw). Longevity practitioners prefer mid-range morning cortisol: high enough to indicate healthy adrenal response but not so high as to suggest chronic stress or Cushing's. Values 15-20 mcg/dL are within normal range but may indicate elevated stress response if consistently at the upper end. |
| **High values indicate** | Cushing's syndrome/disease (pituitary or adrenal tumor), chronic psychological stress, depression, alcoholism, obesity (pseudo-Cushing's), exogenous corticosteroid use, acute illness/trauma, intense exercise, sleep deprivation, oral contraceptives (increase cortisol-binding globulin, raising total cortisol). |
| **Low values indicate** | Adrenal insufficiency (primary = Addison's disease; secondary = pituitary dysfunction), chronic fatigue, adrenal "burnout" from prolonged stress, abrupt corticosteroid withdrawal, hypopituitarism, tuberculosis or other adrenal infections. Symptoms: fatigue, dizziness, hypotension, hypoglycemia, salt craving. |
| **Special considerations** | **MUST be drawn in the morning (before 10 AM), fasting. Afternoon levels are meaningless for comparison due to diurnal rhythm.** Blood draw time MUST be recorded and consistent between measurements. Stress, poor sleep, and intense exercise the day before can elevate results. Oral contraceptives and estrogen therapy raise cortisol-binding globulin, increasing total cortisol without increasing free (active) cortisol -- salivary cortisol or free cortisol may be more accurate in these cases. If morning cortisol is borderline, an ACTH stimulation test is the definitive test for adrenal function. 24-hour urinary free cortisol or late-night salivary cortisol are used for Cushing's screening. |
| **Interactions with other markers** | Interpret alongside **ACTH** (distinguishes primary vs secondary adrenal insufficiency/Cushing's), **DHEA-S** (adrenal androgen; cortisol:DHEA-S ratio reflects adrenal balance), **fasting glucose/insulin** (cortisol is counter-regulatory to insulin), **thyroid hormones** (hypothyroidism can mimic and coexist with cortisol dysfunction), **testosterone** (chronic high cortisol suppresses testosterone), **hs-CRP** (cortisol is anti-inflammatory -- low cortisol may correlate with higher inflammation), **vitamin D** (both regulated by adrenal/immune axis). |

### 14. PSA (Prostate-Specific Antigen)

| Field | Content |
|-------|---------|
| **What it measures** | A protein produced exclusively by prostate epithelial cells. PSA is a serine protease that liquefies semen. Small amounts normally enter the bloodstream. Measured in nanograms per milliliter (ng/mL). PSA is prostate-specific but NOT cancer-specific -- any prostate condition can elevate it. |
| **Why it matters** | Primary screening tool for prostate cancer, the most common non-skin cancer in men. **Baseline at age 40+ is recommended**, with velocity (rate of change over time) being more clinically meaningful than a single absolute number. Early detection of prostate cancer through PSA trends enables timely intervention while cancer is localized and curable. However, PSA has significant limitations in specificity -- elevated PSA triggers biopsies that are often negative or find indolent cancers that may never cause harm. |
| **Standard reference range** | Age-adjusted normal ranges: Age 40-49: 0-2.5 ng/mL. Age 50-59: 0-3.5 ng/mL. Age 60-69: 0-4.5 ng/mL. Age 70-80: 0-5.5 ng/mL. Traditional single-threshold referral cutoff: 4.0 ng/mL (now considered oversimplified). |
| **Optimal range** | for an active adult male: <1.0 ng/mL is ideal and associated with very low long-term prostate cancer risk. A baseline PSA <1.0 ng/mL at age 40-49 predicts very low risk for decades. PSA 1.0-2.5 ng/mL is normal for this age but warrants annual monitoring. **PSA velocity matters more than absolute number**: a rise of >0.4 ng/mL per year (for men with baseline PSA <4) or >0.75 ng/mL per year (for men with PSA 4-10) is concerning and warrants urological evaluation, even if the absolute value is "normal." |
| **High values indicate** | Prostate cancer (but only ~25% of men with PSA 4-10 have cancer on biopsy), benign prostatic hyperplasia (BPH -- very common with aging), prostatitis (infection/inflammation), urinary tract infection, recent ejaculation, recent vigorous exercise (especially cycling), recent prostate manipulation (DRE, biopsy, catheterization), prostate surgery. |
| **Low values indicate** | Normal prostate function. 5-alpha reductase inhibitors (finasteride, dutasteride) reduce PSA by approximately 50% -- the measured value must be doubled for accurate interpretation in men taking these medications. Very low PSA (<0.5 ng/mL) at baseline suggests very low prostate cancer risk. |
| **Special considerations** | **Exercise (especially cycling) can temporarily elevate PSA** -- avoid vigorous exercise and cycling for 48 hours before testing. Avoid ejaculation for 48 hours before testing. Avoid alcohol for 2-3 days before testing. DRE can elevate PSA -- if both are done, draw blood BEFORE the rectal exam. If PSA is elevated, confirmatory tests include: free PSA percentage (low free PSA % is more concerning for cancer), PSA density (PSA / prostate volume), 4Kscore, phi (prostate health index), and MRI before biopsy. Annual PSA tracking for velocity/trend is more valuable than isolated measurements. Black men and those with BRCA2 mutations should start screening at age 40. |
| **Interactions with other markers** | Interpret alongside **free PSA / total PSA ratio** (free PSA <10% of total is more suspicious for cancer; >25% suggests benign disease), **PSA velocity/doubling time** (rate of change over serial measurements), **testosterone** (higher testosterone does NOT increase prostate cancer risk -- this is a debunked myth, but testosterone therapy requires PSA monitoring), **DHT/5-alpha reductase** (5-ARI medications halve PSA), **hs-CRP** (prostatitis can elevate both), **IGF-1** (elevated IGF-1 may be associated with prostate cancer risk in epidemiologic studies). |
