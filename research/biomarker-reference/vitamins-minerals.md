## 1. Vitamin D (25-OH)

| Field | Content |
|-------|---------|
| **What it measures** | 25-hydroxyvitamin D -- the circulating storage form of vitamin D, converted from D2 (ergocalciferol) and D3 (cholecalciferol) by the liver. This is the standard marker for vitamin D status. |
| **Why it matters** | Vitamin D functions as a hormone regulating 200+ genes. It is critical for calcium absorption, bone density, immune regulation (both innate and adaptive), mood/neurotransmitter synthesis, cardiovascular health, cancer risk reduction, and insulin sensitivity. Most people are deficient -- estimates suggest 40-75% of U.S. adults have suboptimal levels. |
| **Standard reference range** | 30-100 ng/mL (conventional "sufficient" cutoff is 30 ng/mL; deficient < 20 ng/mL; insufficient 20-29 ng/mL) |
| **Optimal range** | **40-60 ng/mL** (longevity/functional medicine consensus). Some practitioners (Root FM, GrassrootsHealth scientist panel) target 50-70 ng/mL. Levels of 40+ ng/mL are associated with reduced all-cause mortality; 55+ ng/mL associated with reduced cancer risk. For an active an active adult male: aim for **50-60 ng/mL**. |
| **High values indicate** | Excessive supplementation (toxicity risk above 100 ng/mL, dangerous above 150 ng/mL), granulomatous diseases (sarcoidosis, tuberculosis) that increase 1,25-dihydroxyvitamin D conversion, some lymphomas. Hypercalcemia symptoms: nausea, kidney stones, calcification of soft tissues. |
| **Low values indicate** | Inadequate sun exposure, insufficient dietary intake, malabsorption (celiac, Crohn's, gastric bypass), obesity (vitamin D sequestered in fat tissue), liver or kidney disease, darker skin pigmentation, northern latitude residence, aging (reduced skin synthesis). Associated with increased risk of osteoporosis, autoimmune disease, depression, MS, cardiovascular disease, cancer, and impaired immune function. |
| **Special considerations** | - **Most people are deficient** -- even "active outdoor" males often test 20-35 ng/mL without supplementation. - Supplementation with D3 (cholecalciferol) preferred over D2. - **Take with fat** for absorption (fat-soluble vitamin). - **Vitamin K2 (MK-7)** should be co-supplemented to direct calcium to bones rather than arteries. - **Magnesium** is required for vitamin D metabolism -- deficiency impairs conversion. - Typical maintenance dose: 2,000-5,000 IU/day; repletion may require 7,000-10,000 IU/day. - Retest 8-12 weeks after dose change. - Seasonal variation: levels peak late summer, nadir late winter. - Obese individuals need higher doses (vitamin D is sequestered in adipose tissue). |
| **Interactions with other markers** | - **Calcium & PTH**: Low vitamin D triggers elevated PTH (secondary hyperparathyroidism) which leaches calcium from bones. - **Magnesium**: Required cofactor for vitamin D activation; low Mg can cause apparent vitamin D resistance. - **Phosphorus**: Vitamin D regulates phosphorus absorption. - **hs-CRP**: Low vitamin D associated with higher systemic inflammation. |

> **Key Insight**: The conventional "sufficient" cutoff of 30 ng/mL is a minimum to prevent rickets/osteomalacia -- it is NOT optimal. The gap between 30 and 50 ng/mL represents a zone where many chronic disease risks remain elevated. Target 50-60 ng/mL for a proactive health strategy.

## 2. Vitamin B12

| Field | Content |
|-------|---------|
| **What it measures** | Serum cobalamin (vitamin B12) -- a water-soluble vitamin essential for DNA synthesis, red blood cell formation, neurological function, and methylation pathways. |
| **Why it matters** | B12 is required for methionine synthase (converts homocysteine to methionine) and methylmalonyl-CoA mutase (energy metabolism). Deficiency causes megaloblastic anemia, peripheral neuropathy, cognitive decline, fatigue, and depression. Neurological damage from B12 deficiency can become irreversible if untreated. |
| **Standard reference range** | 200-900 pg/mL (varies by lab; some report 180-914 pg/mL) |
| **Optimal range** | **500-1,000 pg/mL** (functional medicine/longevity consensus). Peter Attia targets >500 pg/mL. OptimalDX recommends 545-1,100 pg/mL. Mito Health and longevity experts target 500-900 pg/mL. For an active an active adult male: aim for **600-900 pg/mL**. |
| **High values indicate** | - Supplementation (most common and benign cause). - Liver disease (hepatitis, cirrhosis -- liver releases stored B12). - Myeloproliferative disorders (polycythemia vera, CML). - Renal failure (impaired clearance). - Elevated haptocorrin. - Levels persistently >1,355 pg/mL without supplementation warrant investigation for malignancy. |
| **Low values indicate** | - Pernicious anemia (autoimmune destruction of intrinsic factor). - Dietary insufficiency (vegetarian/vegan diets). - Malabsorption (celiac, Crohn's, gastric bypass, atrophic gastritis). - Medications: **metformin** (very common -- depletes B12), **PPIs/H2 blockers** (reduce stomach acid needed for absorption), nitrous oxide exposure. - Aging (reduced intrinsic factor and stomach acid production). - Pancreatic insufficiency. |
| **Special considerations** | - **Standard "normal" starts at 200 pg/mL, but many people are functionally deficient at "normal" levels.** A serum B12 of 200-400 pg/mL often masks functional deficiency with symptoms present. - **Methylmalonic acid (MMA)** is a more sensitive marker: MMA > 0.26 umol/L suggests functional B12 deficiency even with "normal" serum B12. - **Homocysteine** also rises with B12 deficiency (target < 8 umol/L). - Supplement forms: methylcobalamin or hydroxocobalamin preferred over cyanocobalamin. - B12 is non-toxic at high supplemental doses (water-soluble, excess excreted). - If on metformin, test B12 at least annually. |
| **Interactions with other markers** | - **Folate**: B12 and folate work together in the methionine/methylation cycle. Folate supplementation can mask B12 deficiency by correcting anemia while neurological damage continues ("folate trap"). Always check both together. - **Homocysteine**: Elevated homocysteine can indicate B12, folate, or B6 deficiency. B12 + folate + homocysteine should be interpreted as a triad. - **MMA (methylmalonic acid)**: The most specific functional marker for B12 status. - **MCV (mean corpuscular volume)**: Elevated MCV (>95 fL) suggests megaloblastic anemia from B12 or folate deficiency. |

> **Key Insight**: A serum B12 of 250 pg/mL is "normal" by lab standards but is almost certainly suboptimal. Many functional medicine practitioners consider anything below 500 pg/mL worth investigating with MMA and homocysteine testing. Do not accept a low-normal B12 without further workup.

## 3. Folate

| Field | Content |
|-------|---------|
| **What it measures** | Serum folate (vitamin B9) -- measures circulating folate levels. RBC folate (if ordered) reflects longer-term tissue stores over 120 days. |
| **Why it matters** | Folate is essential for DNA synthesis and repair, methylation, red blood cell production, and homocysteine metabolism. Deficiency causes megaloblastic anemia, elevated homocysteine (cardiovascular risk), neural tube defects (in offspring), fatigue, cognitive impairment, and mood disturbances. Critical for methylation pathways alongside B12. |
| **Standard reference range** | Serum folate: 2-20 ng/mL (some labs report >3.0 ng/mL as normal). RBC folate: 140-628 ng/mL. |
| **Optimal range** | Serum folate: **15-25 ng/mL** (upper half of range). OptimalDX range: 15-27 ng/mL. RBC folate (preferred test): **1,000-1,500 ng/mL** per Knew Health. WHO-aligned functional minimum: 14 ng/mL serum. For an active an active adult male: aim for **>15 ng/mL serum** or **>1,000 ng/mL RBC folate**. |
| **High values indicate** | - Supplementation (most common). - Possible masking of B12 deficiency (folate corrects anemia but not neurological damage). - Rarely: blind loop syndrome, vegetarian diet. - Very high unmetabolized folic acid (from synthetic supplements) may have adverse effects -- methylfolate (5-MTHF) supplement form preferred. |
| **Low values indicate** | - Dietary insufficiency (inadequate greens, legumes, fortified foods). - Malabsorption (celiac, Crohn's, inflammatory bowel disease). - Alcoholism (folate is one of the first nutrients depleted). - Medications: **methotrexate** (folate antagonist), **phenytoin/anticonvulsants**, **sulfasalazine**, **trimethoprim**, **oral contraceptives**, **metformin**. - Pregnancy/lactation (increased demand). - **MTHFR gene variants** (C677T, A1298C) -- impaired conversion of folate to its active form (5-MTHF). |
| **Special considerations** | - **MTHFR polymorphisms**: ~40% of the population has at least one copy of the C677T variant, reducing folate metabolism efficiency. These individuals benefit from methylfolate (5-MTHF) rather than synthetic folic acid. - Supplement with **methylfolate (5-MTHF)**, not folic acid, especially if MTHFR status is unknown. - Folic acid (synthetic) requires multiple enzymatic conversions; unmetabolized folic acid in serum is a concern at high doses. - RBC folate is a more reliable marker of long-term status than serum folate. - High-dose folate supplementation can mask B12 deficiency -- always check B12 simultaneously. |
| **Interactions with other markers** | - **B12**: Folate and B12 are metabolically linked through the methionine cycle. Folate supplementation without adequate B12 can mask B12 deficiency anemia while neurological damage progresses silently. **Always interpret together.** - **Homocysteine**: Elevated homocysteine may reflect folate, B12, or B6 deficiency. Folate is the primary dietary determinant of homocysteine levels. - **MCV**: Elevated MCV suggests megaloblastic process from folate or B12 deficiency. - **Iron/Ferritin**: Combined folate and iron deficiency can produce a "dimorphic" blood picture where MCV appears normal (macro + micro cancel out). |

## 4. Ferritin

| Field | Content |
|-------|---------|
| **What it measures** | Serum ferritin -- the primary iron storage protein. Each ferritin molecule can store up to 4,500 iron atoms. Reflects total body iron stores under normal conditions. |
| **Why it matters** | Ferritin is the single best screening test for iron deficiency. However, it is also an **acute phase reactant** -- levels rise with inflammation, infection, liver disease, and malignancy, which can mask true iron deficiency. Both iron deficiency (fatigue, anemia, impaired performance) and iron overload (oxidative damage, organ toxicity) are clinically significant. |
| **Standard reference range** | Males: 20-336 ng/mL (varies by lab; Merck Manual: 30-300 ng/mL) |
| **Optimal range** | **60-120 ng/mL** for adult males (Superpower, longevity consensus). CCF Med targets 50-100 ng/mL for men. For an active an active adult male: aim for **75-150 ng/mL** (accounts for higher iron turnover from exercise). Ferritin <30 ng/mL suggests iron depletion even if within "normal" range. |
| **High values indicate** | - **Hemochromatosis** (hereditary iron overload -- surprisingly common in white males; ~1 in 200-300 of Northern European descent are homozygous for HFE C282Y). - Inflammation/infection (acute phase response). - Liver disease (hepatitis, cirrhosis, fatty liver -- damaged hepatocytes release ferritin). - Malignancy (especially hepatocellular carcinoma, lymphoma, leukemia). - Metabolic syndrome/obesity. - Excessive iron supplementation or transfusions. - Chronic alcohol use. - **Ferritin >300 ng/mL in males warrants investigation** regardless of symptoms. |
| **Low values indicate** | - Iron deficiency (the most specific finding -- low ferritin virtually confirms iron depletion). - Chronic blood loss (GI bleeding, heavy exercise-induced hemolysis, frequent blood donation). - Inadequate dietary intake. - Malabsorption. - Celiac disease. - Hypothyroidism (reduces iron absorption). - Ferritin <30 ng/mL can cause fatigue, hair loss, restless legs, and impaired exercise performance even without anemia. |
| **Special considerations** | - **Ferritin is an acute phase reactant** -- it can be elevated by inflammation even when iron stores are actually low. **Always interpret ferritin alongside hs-CRP** (and ideally the full iron panel). If hs-CRP is elevated, ferritin may be falsely normal/high. - In the setting of inflammation, a ferritin <100 ng/mL may actually indicate iron deficiency. - **Iron overload (hemochromatosis) is surprisingly common in white males.** Ferritin + transferrin saturation together screen for this: ferritin >300 ng/mL + transferrin saturation >45% is highly suggestive and warrants HFE gene testing. - Exercise increases ferritin transiently (acute phase response from muscle damage); test in a rested state. - Avoid iron supplements for 24 hours before testing. - Vitamin C enhances iron absorption and can raise ferritin over time. |
| **Interactions with other markers** | - **Iron panel (serum iron, TIBC, transferrin saturation)**: Ferritin must be interpreted alongside the iron panel for a complete picture. High ferritin + high transferrin saturation = iron overload. High ferritin + normal/low transferrin saturation + elevated hs-CRP = inflammation-driven elevation. - **hs-CRP**: Essential companion marker. Elevated CRP signals that ferritin may be artificially elevated. - **Hemoglobin/Hematocrit**: Low Hb with low ferritin confirms iron deficiency anemia. - **Liver enzymes (ALT, AST)**: Elevated with ferritin suggests liver pathology or hemochromatosis with liver involvement. - **GGT**: Elevated GGT + elevated ferritin suggests alcohol-related or metabolic liver disease. |

> **Key Insight**: A ferritin of 250 ng/mL in a male could be normal iron stores, early hemochromatosis, or inflammation-driven elevation. You cannot interpret it without hs-CRP and transferrin saturation. The triad of ferritin + hs-CRP + transferrin saturation is essential for correct interpretation.

## 5. Iron Panel (Serum Iron, TIBC, Transferrin Saturation)

| Field | Content |
|-------|---------|
| **What it measures** | **Serum Iron**: Amount of circulating iron bound to transferrin. **TIBC (Total Iron Binding Capacity)**: The maximum amount of iron that transferrin can carry (indirect measure of transferrin). **Transferrin Saturation (TSAT)**: Percentage of transferrin binding sites occupied by iron (calculated: serum iron / TIBC x 100). |
| **Why it matters** | The iron panel reveals the dynamics of iron transport -- not just stores (ferritin) but how iron is moving through the body. Critical for distinguishing iron deficiency from iron overload, and for differentiating anemia of chronic disease from true iron deficiency. Essential companion to ferritin. |
| **Standard reference range** | **Serum Iron** -- Males: 59-158 mcg/dL (Merck: 75-150 mcg/dL). **TIBC**: 250-450 mcg/dL (some labs: 235-505 mcg/dL). **Transferrin Saturation**: 20-50% for males. |
| **Optimal range** | **Serum Iron**: 80-130 mcg/dL (mid-range). **TIBC**: 275-400 mcg/dL (mid-range). **Transferrin Saturation**: **25-35%** (mid-range; above 45% warrants hemochromatosis investigation). For an active an active adult male: transferrin saturation of **25-35%** is ideal. |
| **High values indicate** | **High Serum Iron + Low TIBC + High TSAT (>45%)**: Hemochromatosis, iron overload, excessive supplementation, hemolytic anemia, liver disease, multiple transfusions. **Transferrin saturation >45%** is the most sensitive early marker for hereditary hemochromatosis -- more sensitive than ferritin in early disease. TSAT >60% strongly suggests hemochromatosis. |
| **Low values indicate** | **Low Serum Iron + High TIBC + Low TSAT (<20%)**: Iron deficiency. **Low Serum Iron + Low/Normal TIBC + Low TSAT**: Anemia of chronic disease/inflammation (iron is being sequestered by hepcidin). **Low TIBC alone**: Malnutrition, liver disease, nephrotic syndrome. |
| **Special considerations** | - **Fasting is recommended** -- serum iron fluctuates significantly with meals (iron-rich meal can increase serum iron 2-3x). Draw fasting, morning sample. - **Diurnal variation**: Serum iron is highest in mid-morning and lowest at night. Test consistently in the morning. - Iron supplements should be withheld 24 hours before testing. - Oral contraceptives and estrogen therapy increase iron and TIBC. - Recent blood transfusion invalidates results. - Serum iron alone is unreliable -- always interpret as a panel. - **Hemolysis** from difficult blood draw falsely elevates serum iron. - Vitamin C intake (supplements) increases iron absorption but does not directly affect serum iron measurement. |
| **Interactions with other markers** | - **Ferritin**: The iron panel + ferritin together give the complete iron status picture. See interpretation patterns below. - **hs-CRP/ESR**: Inflammation causes hepcidin elevation, which traps iron in macrophages -- low serum iron + low TIBC + normal/high ferritin = anemia of chronic disease. - **Hemoglobin, Hematocrit, MCV, MCH**: Iron deficiency anemia shows low Hb, low MCV (microcytic), low MCH. - **Reticulocyte count**: Helps distinguish production vs. destruction anemias. - **HFE gene test**: Ordered when transferrin saturation >45% on repeat testing. |

### Iron Status Interpretation Patterns

| Pattern | Serum Iron | TIBC | TSAT | Ferritin | Interpretation |
|---------|-----------|------|------|----------|----------------|
| Iron deficiency (early) | Normal-low | High | Low (<20%) | Low (<30) | Depleted stores, not yet anemic |
| Iron deficiency anemia | Low | High | Very low (<15%) | Very low (<15) | Depleted stores + anemia |
| Anemia of chronic disease | Low | Low-normal | Low-normal | Normal-high | Inflammation-driven iron sequestration |
| Hemochromatosis | High | Low | High (>45%) | High (>300) | Iron overload |
| Normal iron status | Mid-range | Mid-range | 25-35% | 75-150 | Optimal |

> **Key Insight**: Hemochromatosis is surprisingly common in white males of Northern European descent (~1 in 200-300 are C282Y homozygous). Transferrin saturation >45% on two separate fasting samples warrants HFE gene testing. Early detection prevents organ damage (liver cirrhosis, cardiomyopathy, diabetes, joint disease).

## 6. RBC Magnesium

| Field | Content |
|-------|---------|
| **What it measures** | Magnesium concentration inside red blood cells. Reflects intracellular magnesium status over the lifespan of RBCs (~120 days), providing a more accurate picture of tissue magnesium than serum magnesium. |
| **Why it matters** | Magnesium is a cofactor for 300+ enzymatic reactions including energy production (ATP), protein synthesis, muscle/nerve function, blood sugar regulation, and blood pressure control. It is essential for vitamin D activation, electrolyte balance, and sleep quality. Subclinical magnesium deficiency is estimated to affect up to 50-80% of Americans due to soil depletion, processed food diets, and stress-driven excretion. |
| **Standard reference range** | RBC Magnesium: 4.0-6.4 mg/dL (some labs: 4.2-6.8 mg/dL) |
| **Optimal range** | **6.0-6.8 mg/dL** (functional medicine target per Access Medical Labs and clinical literature). For an active an active adult male with high exercise demands: aim for **6.0-6.5 mg/dL**. Active individuals lose more magnesium through sweat and have higher utilization. |
| **High values indicate** | Excessive supplementation, renal failure (impaired excretion), Addison's disease, hypothyroidism, lithium use, dehydration (hemoconcentration artifact). Symptomatic hypermagnesemia is rare without renal impairment -- usually iatrogenic (IV magnesium). |
| **Low values indicate** | Dietary insufficiency (very common), malabsorption (celiac, IBD, short bowel), chronic stress (cortisol increases urinary Mg excretion), heavy exercise/sweating, alcoholism, diabetes/insulin resistance (glycosuria drives Mg loss), medications: **PPIs** (major culprit -- long-term use depletes Mg), **diuretics**, **certain antibiotics (aminoglycosides)**. Associated with muscle cramps, insomnia, anxiety, arrhythmias, migraines, insulin resistance, hypertension, and osteoporosis. |
| **Special considerations** | - **RBC Magnesium vs. Serum Magnesium**: Serum magnesium (standard test) measures only ~1% of total body magnesium and is tightly homeostatic -- **serum can appear normal even with significant intracellular deficiency** (chronic latent magnesium deficiency). RBC magnesium is the superior test for detecting true deficiency. - Serum Mg reference range is 1.7-2.2 mg/dL; levels below 0.85 mmol/L (2.07 mg/dL) may mask deficiency. - **Avoid magnesium supplements for 24 hours** before testing. - Fasting is generally not required. - Common supplement forms: magnesium glycinate (well-absorbed, calming), magnesium threonate (crosses blood-brain barrier), magnesium malate (energy/muscle), magnesium citrate (GI motility). Avoid magnesium oxide (poor absorption). - Typical supplemental dose: 200-400 mg elemental magnesium/day. - Repletion takes weeks to months due to large intracellular/bone stores. |
| **Interactions with other markers** | - **Vitamin D**: Magnesium is required for vitamin D metabolism (hydroxylation steps). Low Mg can cause apparent vitamin D deficiency or resistance to supplementation. Always optimize Mg when addressing vitamin D. - **Calcium**: Magnesium and calcium compete for absorption and have opposing effects on muscle contraction. Low Mg can cause hypocalcemia. - **Potassium**: Refractory hypokalemia is often caused by underlying magnesium deficiency -- fix Mg first. - **PTH**: Low Mg impairs PTH secretion and action. - **Blood glucose/HbA1c**: Mg improves insulin sensitivity; deficiency worsens insulin resistance. |

> **Key Insight**: If your doctor orders "magnesium" it is almost certainly serum magnesium, which is a poor test. Specifically request **RBC magnesium**. A serum magnesium of 2.0 mg/dL looks "normal" but can coexist with significant intracellular depletion. RBC magnesium is the test that matters.

## 7. Zinc

| Field | Content |
|-------|---------|
| **What it measures** | Serum or plasma zinc concentration -- reflects circulating zinc levels. Zinc is the second most abundant trace mineral in the body (after iron). |
| **Why it matters** | Zinc is a cofactor for 100+ enzymes and is critical for immune function (T-cell maturation, natural killer cell activity), wound healing, protein synthesis, DNA synthesis, testosterone production, taste/smell, and antioxidant defense (superoxide dismutase). Zinc deficiency impairs immune response, delays wound healing, reduces testosterone, and increases susceptibility to infection. |
| **Standard reference range** | 60-130 mcg/dL (some labs: 56-134 mcg/dL; estimated male range: 59-125 mcg/dL) |
| **Optimal range** | **90-130 mcg/dL** (upper half of range). OptimalDX range: 99-130 mcg/dL. For an active an active adult male concerned with immune function and testosterone: aim for **100-120 mcg/dL**. |
| **High values indicate** | Excessive supplementation (most common), contamination of sample (zinc is ubiquitous -- collection technique matters), industrial exposure. Zinc toxicity symptoms: nausea, vomiting, loss of appetite, diarrhea, headaches. Chronic excess depletes copper (zinc and copper compete for absorption via metallothionein). |
| **Low values indicate** | Dietary insufficiency (common in plant-heavy diets due to phytates blocking absorption), malabsorption (celiac, IBD, short bowel), chronic infections/inflammation (zinc is redistributed during acute phase response -- serum drops), liver disease, chronic kidney disease, sickle cell disease, diabetes, alcoholism, heavy exercise/sweating. Associated with impaired immunity, low testosterone, hair loss, poor wound healing, loss of taste/smell, skin lesions. |
| **Special considerations** | - Serum zinc is an imperfect marker -- it drops during acute phase response (infection, inflammation, stress), so a low result during illness may not reflect true deficiency. Interpret alongside hs-CRP/albumin. - **Fasting recommended** -- meals (especially protein-rich) can transiently affect levels. - Draw in the morning (diurnal variation). - **Zinc supplementation depletes copper** -- do not supplement zinc long-term without monitoring copper. The copper:zinc ratio matters (see below). - Zinc competes with iron for absorption -- take at different times. - Phytates (grains, legumes, nuts) inhibit zinc absorption. - Tolerable upper intake: 40 mg/day supplemental zinc for adults. - Common supplement forms: zinc glycinate, zinc picolinate (well absorbed); avoid zinc oxide (poor absorption). |
| **Interactions with other markers** | - **Copper & Ceruloplasmin**: Zinc and copper are metabolic antagonists. The **copper:zinc ratio** (optimal 0.7-1.0) is more informative than either alone. High zinc supplementation without copper monitoring can cause copper deficiency (leading to anemia, neutropenia, neurological issues). - **Testosterone**: Zinc is required for testosterone synthesis. Low zinc correlates with low testosterone. - **Alkaline phosphatase (ALP)**: Zinc-dependent enzyme; low ALP can signal zinc deficiency. - **Albumin**: ~70% of serum zinc is bound to albumin; low albumin falsely lowers serum zinc. - **hs-CRP**: Acute inflammation redistributes zinc; interpret together. |

## 8. Selenium

| Field | Content |
|-------|---------|
| **What it measures** | Serum or plasma selenium concentration -- reflects recent dietary selenium intake. Selenium is an essential trace mineral incorporated into selenoproteins. |
| **Why it matters** | Selenium is a component of 25+ selenoproteins including glutathione peroxidases (antioxidant defense), thioredoxin reductases (redox regulation), and iodothyronine deiodinases (thyroid hormone conversion T4 to T3). Critical for thyroid function, immune defense, cancer prevention, and protection against oxidative stress. **Selenium has a narrow therapeutic window -- both deficiency and excess are harmful.** |
| **Standard reference range** | 70-155 mcg/L (U.S. Institute of Medicine). Mayo Clinic: 110-165 mcg/L for adults. ARUP Labs: 23-190 mcg/L (wide range). |
| **Optimal range** | **110-150 mcg/L** (functional medicine consensus). Levels of ~116 mcg/L associated with reduced all-cause mortality, reduced heart failure risk, and reduced iron deficiency anemia (PREVEND trial). For an active an active adult male: aim for **120-150 mcg/L**. Do NOT push above 150 mcg/L without clinical reason. |
| **High values indicate** | Excessive supplementation (selenosis), occupational/environmental exposure, sample contamination. **Selenium toxicity (selenosis)**: hair loss, brittle/white nails, garlic breath odor, GI distress, peripheral neuropathy, fatigue, irritability. Toxicity risk increases above 400 mcg/day intake (WHO upper limit). Teratogenic effects documented in animal studies. |
| **Low values indicate** | Dietary insufficiency (regionally dependent on soil selenium content), malabsorption, total parenteral nutrition (TPN) without supplementation, chronic kidney disease, HIV/AIDS. Deficiency associated with: **Keshan disease** (cardiomyopathy), **Kashin-Beck disease** (osteoarthropathy), impaired thyroid function (decreased T4 to T3 conversion), compromised immune function, increased cancer risk, increased cardiovascular risk, Hashimoto's thyroiditis exacerbation. Serum selenium <40 mcg/L indicates severe deficiency with loss of glutathione peroxidase activity. |
| **Special considerations** | - **Narrow therapeutic window**: Unlike most vitamins/minerals, selenium toxicity is a real clinical concern. The difference between optimal and toxic is only ~3x (optimal intake ~100-200 mcg/day vs. toxic >400 mcg/day). Do NOT megadose. - Supplementation with 200 mcg/day selenomethionine has shown benefit for Hashimoto's (reduced TPO and TG antibodies) when baseline selenium is <80 mcg/L. - Selenium works synergistically with vitamin E as an antioxidant. - Selenium is volatile -- careful specimen collection required. - Brazil nuts are extremely high in selenium (1 nut = ~70-90 mcg) -- 2-3/day can maintain optimal levels but also cause toxicity if overconsumbed. - Supplement forms: selenomethionine (organic, well-absorbed), sodium selenite (inorganic). |
| **Interactions with other markers** | - **Thyroid panel (TSH, Free T4, Free T3)**: Selenium is required for deiodinase enzymes that convert T4 to T3. Low selenium can cause elevated T4 with low T3 and impaired thyroid function. - **Thyroid antibodies (TPO-Ab, TG-Ab)**: Selenium supplementation reduces antibody levels in autoimmune thyroiditis. - **Glutathione peroxidase**: Functional marker of selenium status. - **Vitamin E**: Works synergistically with selenium in antioxidant defense. - **Mercury**: Selenium binds mercury and may be protective; high mercury exposure can deplete selenium. - **Iron**: Adequate selenium associated with reduced iron deficiency anemia risk (PREVEND trial data). |

> **Key Insight**: Selenium is the trace mineral where "more is NOT better." The optimal range is narrow. A couple of Brazil nuts per day can maintain excellent levels; a handful per day can cause toxicity. If supplementing, 100-200 mcg/day of selenomethionine is the safe range. Always test before and during supplementation.

## 9. Copper

| Field | Content |
|-------|---------|
| **What it measures** | Serum copper (total) -- measures copper bound to ceruloplasmin (~85-95%) plus a small fraction of free (unbound) copper. Copper is an essential trace mineral required for iron metabolism, connective tissue formation, and neurotransmitter synthesis. |
| **Why it matters** | Copper is a cofactor for key enzymes: ceruloplasmin (iron oxidation/transport), cytochrome c oxidase (mitochondrial energy), superoxide dismutase (antioxidant), dopamine beta-hydroxylase (norepinephrine synthesis), lysyl oxidase (collagen/elastin cross-linking). Both deficiency and excess are harmful. Free (unbound) copper is a potent pro-oxidant that causes tissue damage. |
| **Standard reference range** | Males: 70-175 mcg/dL (some labs: 72-166 mcg/dL). Males tend to have lower copper than females (estrogen increases ceruloplasmin). |
| **Optimal range** | **80-120 mcg/dL** for adult males. Copper should be interpreted relative to zinc (copper:zinc ratio). For an active an active adult male: aim for **90-110 mcg/dL** with a copper:zinc ratio of **0.7-1.0**. |
| **High values indicate** | Inflammation/infection (ceruloplasmin is an acute phase reactant -- copper rises with it), estrogen therapy/oral contraceptives, pregnancy, liver disease (acute hepatitis releases copper), malignancy (lymphoma, leukemia), rheumatoid arthritis, thyroid disease. Chronic copper excess: oxidative stress, liver damage, neurological toxicity. Wilson's disease presents with HIGH hepatic copper but often LOW serum copper (due to low ceruloplasmin). |
| **Low values indicate** | Copper deficiency (rare but occurs with: excessive zinc supplementation, malabsorption, gastric bypass, TPN without copper, Menkes disease [genetic]). Manifestations: anemia (refractory to iron), neutropenia, osteoporosis, neurological deficits (myelopathy mimicking B12 deficiency), connective tissue abnormalities. Wilson's disease (paradoxically low total serum copper due to very low ceruloplasmin). |
| **Special considerations** | - **Copper:Zinc ratio is arguably more important than either value alone.** The optimal serum copper:zinc ratio is **0.7-1.0** (calculated using same units, typically mcg/dL). A ratio >1.0 (copper dominant) is associated with increased inflammation, cardiovascular risk, and cognitive decline. - Serum copper rises as an acute phase reactant -- interpret alongside hs-CRP. - **Free copper** (total copper minus ceruloplasmin-bound copper) is the biologically active and potentially toxic fraction. Calculate: Free copper (mcg/dL) = Total copper (mcg/dL) - (3.15 x Ceruloplasmin in mg/dL). Normal free copper: 10-15 mcg/dL. Elevated free copper suggests copper toxicity or Wilson's disease. - Zinc supplementation >40 mg/day without copper co-supplementation can cause iatrogenic copper deficiency -- a significant and underrecognized risk. - Copper pipes in older homes can contribute to copper intake via drinking water. |
| **Interactions with other markers** | - **Ceruloplasmin**: Must be ordered alongside copper to calculate free copper and distinguish bound vs. unbound copper. Ceruloplasmin is also an acute phase reactant. - **Zinc**: Metabolic antagonist. Copper:zinc ratio (0.7-1.0 optimal) is a key health indicator. Supplementing one without monitoring the other can create dangerous imbalances. - **Iron/Ferritin**: Ceruloplasmin (copper-dependent) is required to oxidize Fe2+ to Fe3+ for iron transport. Copper deficiency can cause iron deficiency anemia that is refractory to iron supplementation. - **hs-CRP**: Copper and ceruloplasmin are both acute phase reactants; elevated CRP means copper may be artificially elevated. |

## 10. Ceruloplasmin

| Field | Content |
|-------|---------|
| **What it measures** | Serum ceruloplasmin -- a copper-containing glycoprotein (alpha-2 globulin) synthesized by the liver. Each molecule binds 6 copper atoms. It carries 85-95% of circulating copper and functions as a ferroxidase (oxidizes Fe2+ to Fe3+ for transport by transferrin). |
| **Why it matters** | Ceruloplasmin is the primary copper transport protein and essential for iron metabolism. It is the key diagnostic marker for Wilson's disease (low ceruloplasmin) and is needed to calculate free copper levels. As an acute phase reactant, it also provides information about inflammatory status. |
| **Standard reference range** | 20-40 mg/dL (some labs: 14-40 mg/dL; EBM Consult: 27-37 mg/dL for adults; ScienceDirect: 200-600 mg/L, i.e., 20-60 mg/dL) |
| **Optimal range** | **25-35 mg/dL** (mid-range). For an active an active adult male: aim for **25-35 mg/dL**. Values should be interpreted alongside copper and zinc to calculate the copper:zinc ratio and free copper. |
| **High values indicate** | Inflammation/infection (acute phase reactant), pregnancy, estrogen therapy/oral contraceptives, smoking, lymphoma, rheumatoid arthritis, liver disease (active hepatitis), hyperthyroidism. Elevated ceruloplasmin is a nonspecific marker of systemic inflammation. |
| **Low values indicate** | **Wilson's disease** (classic finding: ceruloplasmin <20 mg/dL, often <10 mg/dL; present in ~90% of Wilson's patients). Menkes disease (X-linked copper transport disorder). Severe liver disease/hepatic failure (reduced synthesis). Nephrotic syndrome (urinary protein loss). Malabsorption/malnutrition. Protein-losing enteropathy. Aceruloplasminemia (rare genetic disorder). Note: 10-20% of Wilson's disease gene heterozygotes also have low ceruloplasmin but do NOT require treatment. |
| **Special considerations** | - Ceruloplasmin is an **acute phase reactant** -- it can be falsely elevated during infection or inflammation, potentially masking Wilson's disease (false-normal ceruloplasmin in an inflamed Wilson's patient). - Neonatal ceruloplasmin is normally low (50-180 mg/L); adult levels reached by adolescence. - **Free copper calculation**: Free copper (mcg/dL) = Total copper (mcg/dL) - (3.15 x Ceruloplasmin mg/dL). Normal free copper = 10-15 mcg/dL. Free copper >25 mcg/dL is concerning for copper toxicity or Wilson's disease. - Any protein deficiency state can lower ceruloplasmin independent of copper status. - Wilson's disease, while rare (~1 in 30,000), is treatable and fatal if untreated. Low ceruloplasmin + neuropsychiatric symptoms + liver disease + Kayser-Fleischer rings (corneal copper deposits) = classic presentation. |
| **Interactions with other markers** | - **Copper (serum total)**: Ceruloplasmin + total copper together allow calculation of free copper. This triad (ceruloplasmin, total copper, free copper) is the gold standard for copper status assessment. - **Zinc**: Part of the copper-zinc-ceruloplasmin triad that should be evaluated together. The copper:zinc ratio (optimal 0.7-1.0) integrates information from all three. - **Iron/Ferritin/Transferrin**: Ceruloplasmin is a ferroxidase required for iron transport. Low ceruloplasmin can impair iron utilization, causing anemia that looks like iron deficiency but does not respond to iron supplementation. - **Liver function tests (ALT, AST, bilirubin, albumin)**: In Wilson's disease, ceruloplasmin is low alongside elevated liver enzymes and potentially low albumin. - **24-hour urine copper**: Companion test for Wilson's disease diagnosis (elevated >100 mcg/day in Wilson's). |

## Cross-Test Interpretation Clusters

The tests above should not be interpreted in isolation. Here are the key clusters:

### Cluster 1: Iron Status
- **Ferritin + Iron Panel (Serum Iron, TIBC, TSAT) + hs-CRP + CBC**
- Ferritin alone is insufficient. The iron panel distinguishes deficiency from overload. hs-CRP distinguishes true iron stores from inflammation-driven ferritin elevation.
- Hemochromatosis screen: Ferritin >300 ng/mL + TSAT >45% in a white male = order HFE gene test.

### Cluster 2: Methylation / B-Vitamin Status
- **B12 + Folate + Homocysteine (+/- MMA, MCV)**
- These three form the methylation triad. Elevated homocysteine with normal B12 and folate suggests B6 deficiency. Low B12 + low folate + high homocysteine = combined deficiency. Normal B12 but elevated MMA = functional/intracellular B12 deficiency.

### Cluster 3: Copper-Zinc Balance
- **Copper + Ceruloplasmin + Zinc**
- Calculate copper:zinc ratio (optimal 0.7-1.0, same units mcg/dL).
- Calculate free copper: Total copper - (3.15 x ceruloplasmin). Normal: 10-15 mcg/dL.
- An elevated copper:zinc ratio (>1.0) is a marker of inflammation, oxidative stress, and increased disease risk.

### Cluster 4: Mineral Cofactors for Vitamin D
- **Vitamin D + RBC Magnesium + Calcium + PTH**
- Magnesium is required for vitamin D activation. If vitamin D is low despite supplementation, check RBC magnesium first. Low vitamin D + elevated PTH = secondary hyperparathyroidism driving bone loss.

### Cluster 5: Thyroid-Selenium Connection
- **Selenium + TSH + Free T4 + Free T3 + TPO Antibodies**
- Low selenium impairs T4-to-T3 conversion and exacerbates autoimmune thyroiditis. If Free T3 is low relative to Free T4, check selenium.
