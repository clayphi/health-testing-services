## THYROID PANEL

> **Critical note:** TSH alone is insufficient for thyroid assessment. A full panel (TSH + Free T3 + Free T4) is required because TSH can be normal while T3/T4 are suboptimal. TSH measures pituitary signaling only -- not whether T4 converts properly to active T3 at the cellular level. A patient can have normal TSH alongside low Free T3 from impaired conversion driven by stress, iron deficiency, or selenium deficiency.

---

### 1. TSH (Thyroid Stimulating Hormone)

| Field | Content |
|-------|---------|
| **What it measures** | Pituitary gland output of thyroid-stimulating hormone. TSH signals the thyroid to produce T4 and T3. It is an inverse marker -- high TSH means the thyroid is underperforming, low TSH means excess thyroid hormone is detected. |
| **Why it matters** | Primary screening marker for thyroid dysfunction. Drives the feedback loop that governs metabolic rate, energy, body temperature, heart rate, cognition, and mood. Subclinical thyroid dysfunction affects cardiovascular risk, lipid metabolism, and longevity. |
| **Standard reference range** | 0.45 -- 4.5 mIU/L (LabCorp/Quest) |
| **Optimal range** | **1.0 -- 2.0 mIU/L.** Functional medicine consensus and longevity-focused practitioners (Attia, Function Health) target this narrower band. The standard upper limit of 4.5 was derived from population averages that included individuals with undiagnosed thyroid disease, inflating what appears "normal." |
| **High values indicate** | Primary hypothyroidism (most commonly Hashimoto's thyroiditis), iodine deficiency, thyroid damage/surgical removal, recovery phase after thyroiditis, lithium or amiodarone use. Subclinical hypothyroidism: TSH 4.5--10 with normal Free T4. |
| **Low values indicate** | Hyperthyroidism (Graves' disease, toxic nodule, thyroiditis), excessive thyroid medication, pituitary dysfunction (secondary hypothyroidism -- rare), high-dose biotin supplement interference (falsely low). Non-thyroidal illness (sick euthyroid syndrome). |
| **Special considerations** | **Biotin interference:** Biotin supplements cause falsely LOW TSH and falsely HIGH T3/T4 on streptavidin-biotin immunoassays, mimicking hyperthyroidism. Stop biotin for at least 2--3 days (conservatively 5--7 days) before testing. **Timing:** TSH has a diurnal rhythm -- highest in early morning (2--4 AM peak), lowest in late afternoon. Draw before 10 AM for consistency. **Fasting:** Fasting slightly raises TSH; eating lowers it. Be consistent across serial measurements. **Exercise:** Acute intense exercise can transiently lower TSH. **Medications:** Glucocorticoids, dopamine, metformin can lower TSH. Lithium, amiodarone raise it. |
| **Interactions with other markers** | Always interpret with **Free T3 + Free T4**. TSH + normal Free T3/T4 = subclinical dysfunction. Also interpret with **TPO/TgAb antibodies** (autoimmune cause). **Reverse T3** (rT3) adds conversion context. If TSH is normal but symptoms persist, check Free T3 specifically -- impaired T4-to-T3 conversion is a common functional issue. |

### 2. Free T3

| Field | Content |
|-------|---------|
| **What it measures** | Unbound (bioavailable) triiodothyronine -- the most metabolically active thyroid hormone. Approximately 80% of T3 is produced by peripheral conversion of T4 via deiodinase enzymes; only ~20% is secreted directly by the thyroid. |
| **Why it matters** | T3 is the "active" thyroid hormone at the cellular level. It directly regulates metabolic rate, mitochondrial function, body temperature, heart rate, muscle protein synthesis, cognitive function, and mood. Free T3 reveals whether the body is effectively converting T4 to its active form. |
| **Standard reference range** | 2.0 -- 4.4 pg/mL (LabCorp); 2.3 -- 4.2 pg/mL (Quest) |
| **Optimal range** | **3.0 -- 4.0 pg/mL.** For an active adult optimizing for longevity, mid-to-upper range Free T3 supports energy, cognition, and lean body composition without pushing into hyperthyroid territory. Some functional practitioners target 3.2 -- 3.8 pg/mL as the sweet spot. |
| **High values indicate** | Hyperthyroidism (Graves' disease, toxic nodule), T3 thyrotoxicosis (elevated T3 with normal T4 -- can be early Graves'), excessive T3 supplementation (liothyronine/Cytomel), acute thyroiditis (hormone dump). |
| **Low values indicate** | Hypothyroidism, poor T4-to-T3 conversion (common in chronic stress, selenium/zinc/iron deficiency, caloric restriction, illness), non-thyroidal illness syndrome ("sick euthyroid"), excessive reverse T3 production. Low T3 with normal TSH is a red flag for conversion issues. |
| **Special considerations** | **Biotin interference:** Falsely ELEVATED Free T3 on biotin-based immunoassays -- stop biotin 2--7 days before testing. **Conversion factors:** Selenium, zinc, and iron are cofactors for deiodinase enzymes; deficiency impairs T4-to-T3 conversion. **Caloric restriction/fasting:** Prolonged fasting or extreme caloric deficit reduces T4-to-T3 conversion as a protective mechanism. **Stress:** Chronic cortisol elevation shunts T4 toward reverse T3 instead of T3. **Timing:** Best drawn in morning for consistency; T3 has slight diurnal variation. |
| **Interactions with other markers** | Interpret with **TSH + Free T4 + Reverse T3**. Low Free T3 with normal Free T4 = conversion problem. High Reverse T3 with low Free T3 = stress/illness-driven shunting. Check **iron/ferritin, selenium, zinc** if conversion is poor. Ratio of Free T3 to Reverse T3 (>0.2 when both in pg/mL and ng/dL respectively) is used by some practitioners. |

### 3. Free T4

| Field | Content |
|-------|---------|
| **What it measures** | Unbound (bioavailable) thyroxine -- the primary hormone produced by the thyroid gland. T4 is the "storage" or "prohormone" form that circulates in the blood and is converted to active T3 in peripheral tissues (liver, kidneys, brain, muscle). |
| **Why it matters** | Reflects direct thyroid gland output. Low Free T4 confirms hypothyroidism; high Free T4 confirms hyperthyroidism. Critical for distinguishing between thyroid production problems vs. peripheral conversion problems (which Free T3 reveals). |
| **Standard reference range** | 0.82 -- 1.77 ng/dL (LabCorp); 0.8 -- 1.8 ng/dL (Quest) |
| **Optimal range** | **1.0 -- 1.5 ng/dL.** Mid-range Free T4 ensures adequate substrate for T3 conversion without indicating overproduction. Some functional practitioners target the upper half of the range (1.2 -- 1.5 ng/dL) for active individuals. |
| **High values indicate** | Hyperthyroidism (Graves', toxic nodule, thyroiditis), excessive levothyroxine dose, acute thyroiditis (hormone release from damaged gland), factitious thyrotoxicosis. Biotin supplementation can cause falsely high results. |
| **Low values indicate** | Primary hypothyroidism (Hashimoto's, iodine deficiency, thyroid damage), secondary hypothyroidism (pituitary dysfunction -- low TSH + low Free T4), tertiary hypothyroidism (hypothalamic dysfunction). |
| **Special considerations** | **Biotin interference:** Falsely ELEVATED Free T4 on biotin-based assays -- same precautions as TSH/T3 (stop 2--7 days before draw). **Pregnancy/estrogen:** Elevated estrogen (exogenous or pregnancy) increases TBG (thyroxine-binding globulin), which can affect total T4 but not Free T4 -- this is why Free T4 is preferred over Total T4. **Medications:** Phenytoin, carbamazepine, and high-dose salicylates can displace T4 from binding proteins, transiently raising Free T4. Amiodarone can raise or lower T4 depending on the phase of its thyroid effects. |
| **Interactions with other markers** | Always interpret with **TSH + Free T3**. Normal Free T4 with low Free T3 = conversion problem (not a production problem). Low Free T4 with high TSH = primary hypothyroidism. Low Free T4 with low/normal TSH = secondary (pituitary) hypothyroidism. Check **TPO/TgAb antibodies** if hypothyroidism is confirmed to identify autoimmune cause. |

### 4. TPO Antibodies (Thyroid Peroxidase)

| Field | Content |
|-------|---------|
| **What it measures** | Antibodies against thyroid peroxidase, an enzyme essential for thyroid hormone synthesis. TPO antibodies indicate autoimmune attack on the thyroid gland. |
| **Why it matters** | The primary marker for Hashimoto's thyroiditis (autoimmune hypothyroidism), the most common cause of hypothyroidism. Elevated TPO antibodies can be present years before TSH becomes abnormal, making this an early detection marker. Also found in Graves' disease (~75% of cases). |
| **Standard reference range** | < 34 IU/mL (LabCorp); < 9 IU/mL (Quest, varies by assay) |
| **Optimal range** | **< 15 IU/mL, ideally undetectable (0).** Any measurable TPO antibodies indicate some degree of immune activation against the thyroid. Functional practitioners aim for levels as close to zero as possible. |
| **High values indicate** | Hashimoto's thyroiditis (most common), Graves' disease, postpartum thyroiditis, other autoimmune conditions (type 1 diabetes, rheumatoid arthritis -- autoimmune clustering). Levels > 500 IU/mL strongly suggest active Hashimoto's. Mildly elevated levels (35--100) may indicate early or subclinical autoimmune thyroiditis. |
| **Low values indicate** | Absence of autoimmune thyroid disease (though ~5--10% of Hashimoto's patients are antibody-negative). |
| **Special considerations** | **Prevalence:** ~10--15% of the general population has elevated TPO antibodies, many without overt thyroid dysfunction. **Progression:** Positive TPO antibodies confer a 2--4% annual risk of progressing to overt hypothyroidism. **Biotin:** Less affected than TSH/T3/T4, but can still interfere depending on assay platform -- stop biotin before testing as a precaution. **Lifestyle factors:** Gluten sensitivity, gut permeability, selenium deficiency, and chronic inflammation may exacerbate autoimmune thyroid activity. **Selenium supplementation:** 200 mcg/day of selenomethionine has been shown in multiple trials to reduce TPO antibodies. |
| **Interactions with other markers** | Interpret alongside **TgAb** (some patients are TPO-negative but TgAb-positive). Correlate with **TSH, Free T3, Free T4** to determine functional impact. Rising antibodies with worsening TSH = progressive autoimmune destruction. Check **Vitamin D, selenium, ferritin** as deficiencies are associated with higher antibody levels. |

### 5. TgAb (Thyroglobulin Antibodies)

| Field | Content |
|-------|---------|
| **What it measures** | Antibodies against thyroglobulin, the protein scaffold used by the thyroid to synthesize T3 and T4. Like TPO antibodies, TgAb indicates autoimmune activity targeting the thyroid. |
| **Why it matters** | Complements TPO antibodies for autoimmune thyroid disease detection. Some Hashimoto's patients are TPO-negative but TgAb-positive (~3--5% of cases). Also critical in thyroid cancer surveillance -- TgAb interfere with thyroglobulin tumor marker assays. |
| **Standard reference range** | < 20 IU/mL (LabCorp); < 1 IU/mL (Quest, varies by assay) |
| **Optimal range** | **< 10 IU/mL, ideally undetectable.** As with TPO antibodies, any measurable level suggests some immune response against thyroid tissue. |
| **High values indicate** | Hashimoto's thyroiditis, Graves' disease (less common than TPO), differentiated thyroid cancer (papillary or follicular), postpartum thyroiditis. Can also be transiently elevated in subacute thyroiditis. |
| **Low values indicate** | Absence of autoimmune thyroglobulin response. Does not rule out other forms of thyroid disease. |
| **Special considerations** | **Thyroid cancer monitoring:** TgAb are clinically important because they interfere with thyroglobulin (Tg) measurement. In thyroid cancer patients, rising TgAb may itself serve as a surrogate tumor marker. **Prevalence:** Found in ~10% of the general population. **Assay variability:** Reference ranges vary significantly between labs and assay methods. **Trend matters more than single value:** Serial measurements showing rising titers are more concerning than a single mildly elevated result. |
| **Interactions with other markers** | Interpret with **TPO antibodies** (together they provide the full autoimmune thyroid picture). If both are negative but thyroid dysfunction is present, consider non-autoimmune causes (iodine excess/deficiency, medications, pituitary issues). In thyroid cancer patients, always pair with **thyroglobulin (Tg)** levels. Correlate with **TSH, Free T3, Free T4** for functional status. |

## SEX HORMONES

> **Critical note for testosterone testing:** Morning draws are essential for men under 45. Testosterone levels follow a strong diurnal rhythm -- peak levels occur between 7--9 AM and can drop 25--50% by afternoon. For an active adult, draw before 10 AM, fasting, for the most reliable results. All serial measurements should be drawn at the same time of day.

> **SHBG context:** High SHBG means less bioavailable testosterone even if total T looks normal. Total testosterone alone can be misleading -- always interpret alongside Free Testosterone and SHBG. Two men with identical total T of 600 ng/dL can have vastly different free T if one has SHBG of 20 and the other has SHBG of 60.

---

### 6. Total Testosterone

| Field | Content |
|-------|---------|
| **What it measures** | The total concentration of testosterone in serum, including all three fractions: SHBG-bound (~65--70%), albumin-bound (~25--30%), and free/unbound (~1--3%). Measured by immunoassay or LC-MS/MS (mass spectrometry, the gold standard). |
| **Why it matters** | Primary androgen in males. Governs muscle mass, bone density, fat distribution, libido, erectile function, mood, cognitive function, red blood cell production, and cardiovascular health. Declining testosterone is associated with increased all-cause mortality, metabolic syndrome, cardiovascular disease, osteoporosis, and cognitive decline. |
| **Standard reference range** | 264 -- 916 ng/dL (LabCorp, harmonized); 250 -- 1100 ng/dL (Quest). Note: these ranges include all adult males age 18--100+, including those with undiagnosed conditions. |
| **Optimal range** | **500 -- 900 ng/dL.** For an active adult optimizing for longevity: Peter Attia targets 400--700 ng/dL for TRT patients but emphasizes symptom resolution over numbers. For natural production in an active male, 500--900 represents the upper-normal range associated with best outcomes for body composition, cardiovascular health, and cognitive performance. Below 500 warrants investigation; below 400 is likely symptomatic. |
| **High values indicate** | Exogenous testosterone use (TRT, anabolic steroids), testicular tumors (rare), adrenal tumors (rare), congenital adrenal hyperplasia, hyperthyroidism (increases SHBG and total T). Supraphysiologic levels (>1100) carry cardiovascular and hematologic risks (polycythemia). |
| **Low values indicate** | Primary hypogonadism (testicular failure -- Klinefelter's, trauma, varicocele, orchitis), secondary hypogonadism (pituitary/hypothalamic dysfunction -- tumor, medications, obesity), aging (1--2% annual decline after age 30), obesity/metabolic syndrome, chronic opioid use, chronic illness, sleep deprivation, overtraining, excessive alcohol, chronic stress/elevated cortisol. |
| **Special considerations** | **Morning draw essential:** Draw between 7--10 AM fasting. Levels can drop 25--50% by afternoon in men under 45. **Fasting:** Eating (especially glucose load) acutely suppresses testosterone by 25% for 1--2 hours. **Sleep:** Poor sleep (< 5 hrs) can reduce testosterone by 10--15%. **Obesity:** Every 1-point BMI increase is associated with a 2% decline in testosterone. **Assay method:** LC-MS/MS is preferred over immunoassay for accuracy, especially at low levels. **Two low readings required:** Endocrine Society guidelines require two morning total T readings below threshold on separate days to diagnose hypogonadism. |
| **Interactions with other markers** | **Must interpret with Free Testosterone + SHBG.** High SHBG inflates total T while free T is low (functional deficiency). Also interpret with **LH + FSH** (primary vs. secondary hypogonadism), **Estradiol** (aromatization), **Prolactin** (if very low T, to rule out prolactinoma), **CBC/Hematocrit** (polycythemia risk on TRT), **PSA** (prostate monitoring on TRT). |

### 7. Free Testosterone

| Field | Content |
|-------|---------|
| **What it measures** | The unbound fraction of testosterone (~1--3% of total) that is immediately bioavailable to tissues. Can be measured directly by equilibrium dialysis (gold standard), by direct immunoassay (less accurate), or calculated from Total T, SHBG, and albumin using the Vermeulen equation. |
| **Why it matters** | Free testosterone is the fraction that actually enters cells and activates androgen receptors. It is the most clinically relevant measure of androgen status. A man can have "normal" total testosterone but functionally low free testosterone if SHBG is elevated -- this is a common finding in lean, active men and those with higher estrogen or thyroid hormone levels. Per Peter Attia, free testosterone should be approximately 2% of total testosterone. |
| **Standard reference range** | 35 -- 155 pg/mL (direct immunoassay, Quest); 46 -- 224 pg/mL (calculated, LabCorp varies by age). Equilibrium dialysis gold standard: 66 -- 309 pg/mL (Endocrine Society harmonized). Note: ranges vary significantly by method. |
| **Optimal range** | **100 -- 225 pg/mL (calculated) or targeting ~2% of Total T.** For an active adult, free T in the 100--200 pg/mL range (calculated method) corresponds to robust androgen signaling for muscle preservation, cognition, libido, and longevity. If Total T is 700 ng/dL, free T should be approximately 14 ng/dL (~140 pg/mL) at the 2% target. |
| **High values indicate** | Exogenous testosterone/anabolics, low SHBG (obesity, insulin resistance, hypothyroidism), androgen-secreting tumors. Very high free T with low SHBG may reflect metabolic dysfunction rather than true androgen excess. |
| **Low values indicate** | Same causes as low total T, plus: elevated SHBG (aging, hyperthyroidism, liver disease, estrogen excess, certain medications), even when total T appears normal. This is the most common "hidden" hypogonadism pattern in lean, active men. |
| **Special considerations** | **Measurement method matters enormously.** Equilibrium dialysis is the gold standard but expensive and slow. Calculated free T (Vermeulen equation using Total T + SHBG + albumin) is clinically adequate and most commonly used. Direct immunoassay for free T is considered unreliable by the Endocrine Society and should be avoided. **Morning draw:** Same timing requirements as total T -- 7--10 AM, fasting. **SHBG context:** Always interpret free T in the context of SHBG. A free T of 80 pg/mL with SHBG of 50 nmol/L means something different than 80 pg/mL with SHBG of 20 nmol/L. |
| **Interactions with other markers** | **Triad: Total T + Free T + SHBG** -- these three together give the complete androgenic picture. Also correlate with **Estradiol** (high E2 raises SHBG, lowering free T), **Thyroid panel** (hyperthyroidism raises SHBG), **Insulin/glucose** (insulin resistance lowers SHBG, raising free T but indicating metabolic dysfunction), **Albumin** (needed for calculated free T). |

### 8. Estradiol (E2)

| Field | Content |
|-------|---------|
| **What it measures** | The primary estrogen in males, produced by aromatization of testosterone in adipose tissue, brain, bone, and other tissues via the aromatase enzyme. In males, estradiol plays essential roles in bone density, cardiovascular health, libido, cognitive function, and joint health. |
| **Why it matters** | **Estradiol in males has a narrow optimal window -- both too high and too low cause significant problems.** It is not simply a "female hormone" to be minimized. Appropriate estradiol is essential for bone health (prevents osteoporosis), cardiovascular protection, libido, erectile function, and cognitive function. Excessive estradiol causes gynecomastia, water retention, mood disturbances, and blunts testosterone's effects. |
| **Standard reference range** | < 39 pg/mL (standard immunoassay, Quest); 8 -- 35 pg/mL (sensitive LC-MS/MS assay, LabCorp). Note: the sensitive (LC-MS/MS) assay is strongly preferred for males -- standard immunoassays are unreliable at low male-range levels. |
| **Optimal range** | **20 -- 35 pg/mL (sensitive assay).** For an active adult: the 20--35 range balances bone/cardiovascular/cognitive benefits against estrogen-excess symptoms. Below 20 pg/mL risks bone loss and joint pain. Above 40 pg/mL is associated with gynecomastia, water retention, and mood issues in many men. Some TRT-focused clinicians use 20--30 pg/mL as the tighter target. |
| **High values indicate** | Excess aromatization (obesity/high body fat is the primary driver), exogenous testosterone (more substrate for aromatase), liver dysfunction (impaired estrogen clearance), testicular tumors (rare), HCG use, medications (certain antifungals). Symptoms: gynecomastia, water retention/bloating, mood swings, erectile dysfunction, reduced libido. |
| **Low values indicate** | Over-aggressive aromatase inhibitor use (most common iatrogenic cause), very low body fat, low testosterone (less substrate), zinc excess, certain medications. Symptoms: joint pain/stiffness, bone loss/osteoporosis risk, fatigue, low libido (paradoxically similar to high E2), dry skin, mood changes. **Low E2 (<10 pg/mL) is strongly linked to fractures and accelerated bone loss.** |
| **Special considerations** | **Assay type is critical:** Always use the **sensitive LC-MS/MS assay** (sometimes called "ultrasensitive" or "by LCMS") for males. Standard immunoassays designed for female ranges are unreliable at male-range concentrations. **Aromatase inhibitor caution:** Over-suppressing estradiol (a common mistake in TRT management) causes more problems than it solves. Crashing E2 leads to joint pain, bone loss, fatigue, and depression. **Body composition:** Adipose tissue is the primary site of aromatization -- reducing body fat is the most effective long-term strategy for managing elevated E2. **Timing:** Less diurnal variation than testosterone, but morning draw is still preferred for consistency. |
| **Interactions with other markers** | **Testosterone:Estradiol ratio** is clinically informative -- target ratio of roughly 15:1 to 25:1 (Total T in ng/dL : E2 in pg/mL). Interpret alongside **Total T + Free T** (more T = more substrate for aromatization), **SHBG** (low SHBG + high E2 = metabolic pattern), **Body fat percentage** (primary driver of aromatization), **Liver function tests** (impaired clearance), **CBC/Hematocrit** (in TRT context). |

### 9. SHBG (Sex Hormone-Binding Globulin)

| Field | Content |
|-------|---------|
| **What it measures** | A glycoprotein produced by the liver that binds sex hormones (primarily testosterone and estradiol) in the blood, regulating their bioavailability. SHBG-bound hormones are biologically inactive -- only free and albumin-bound fractions are bioavailable. |
| **Why it matters** | SHBG is the key contextual marker for interpreting testosterone results. **High SHBG means less bioavailable testosterone even if total T looks normal.** Conversely, low SHBG means more free hormone but often signals metabolic dysfunction. SHBG is increasingly recognized as an independent marker for metabolic health, insulin sensitivity, and cardiovascular risk. |
| **Standard reference range** | 10 -- 57 nmol/L (LabCorp); 13.3 -- 89.5 nmol/L (Quest, varies). Ranges are broad and age-dependent -- SHBG rises ~1--2% per year after age 40. |
| **Optimal range** | **20 -- 45 nmol/L.** For an active adult: mid-range SHBG (25--40 nmol/L) balances adequate free testosterone availability with metabolic health signaling. Below 20 nmol/L often indicates insulin resistance or metabolic syndrome. Above 50 nmol/L may cause functional testosterone deficiency despite normal total T. |
| **High values indicate** | Aging, hyperthyroidism, liver disease (cirrhosis, hepatitis), estrogen excess, anticonvulsants (phenytoin), HIV, anorexia/low caloric intake, endurance exercise. Clinically: normal total T with low free T and hypogonadal symptoms despite "good" lab numbers. |
| **Low values indicate** | Insulin resistance/type 2 diabetes (strong inverse correlation), obesity, hypothyroidism, nephrotic syndrome, PCOS (females), exogenous androgens/anabolics, high-dose glucocorticoids. Clinically: higher free T but often in the context of metabolic dysfunction -- not a healthy state. |
| **Special considerations** | **Insulin is the primary regulator of SHBG.** High insulin (insulin resistance) suppresses hepatic SHBG production -- this is why SHBG is used as a surrogate marker for insulin sensitivity. **Weight loss and exercise** (especially resistance training) improve insulin sensitivity and normalize SHBG. **Thyroid status:** Hyperthyroidism significantly raises SHBG; hypothyroidism lowers it. Correct thyroid dysfunction before interpreting SHBG. **Diet:** Very low-carb diets may raise SHBG; high-protein diets may lower it. **Medications:** Oral estrogens and anticonvulsants raise SHBG. Exogenous testosterone and glucocorticoids lower it. |
| **Interactions with other markers** | **Core triad: Total T + Free T + SHBG** -- never interpret testosterone without SHBG context. Also interpret with **Estradiol** (SHBG binds E2 too), **Fasting insulin/HOMA-IR** (insulin resistance connection), **Thyroid panel** (T4/T3 status affects SHBG), **Liver function tests** (hepatic production), **Albumin** (needed for free T calculation). |

### 10. DHEA-S

| Field | Content |
|-------|---------|
| **What it measures** | Dehydroepiandrosterone sulfate, the sulfated form of DHEA. Produced almost exclusively by the adrenal glands (~90--95%), with small contributions from the testes. It is the most abundant circulating steroid hormone and serves as a precursor to both testosterone and estrogen. |
| **Why it matters** | DHEA-S is the primary marker of adrenal androgen output and a biomarker of adrenal aging ("adrenopause"). Levels peak in the mid-20s and decline ~2--3% per year thereafter. Low DHEA-S is associated with increased all-cause mortality, cardiovascular disease, cognitive decline, depression, and reduced immune function in aging men. It is also a marker of overall HPA-axis health and adrenal reserve. |
| **Standard reference range** | 45 -- 345 mcg/dL (males 40--49, LabCorp); 32 -- 240 mcg/dL (some labs). Note: ranges are highly age-dependent and decline significantly with each decade. |
| **Optimal range** | **200 -- 350 mcg/dL.** For an active adult targeting longevity: upper-quartile for age group. Some longevity practitioners aim to maintain DHEA-S levels comparable to a healthy 25--35 year old (250--400 mcg/dL) through supplementation if naturally low. Below 150 mcg/dL In an active adult warrants investigation. |
| **High values indicate** | Congenital adrenal hyperplasia (CAH), adrenal tumors (adrenocortical carcinoma), PCOS (females), exogenous DHEA supplementation, Cushing's syndrome (adrenal origin). Very high levels (>600 mcg/dL) without supplementation warrant adrenal imaging. |
| **Low values indicate** | Adrenal insufficiency (Addison's disease), chronic stress/adrenal fatigue (HPA-axis suppression), long-term glucocorticoid use (suppresses adrenal function), hypopituitarism, aging (physiologic decline), chronic illness, anorexia. |
| **Special considerations** | **Stable throughout the day:** Unlike cortisol and testosterone, DHEA-S has minimal diurnal variation, making it a convenient and reliable marker to draw at any time. **No fasting required.** **Long half-life:** Changes slowly (weeks to months), so serial measurements should be spaced at least 4--8 weeks apart. **Supplementation:** Over-the-counter DHEA supplements (25--50 mg/day) reliably raise DHEA-S levels. Monitor for downstream androgen/estrogen effects. **Not a standalone marker:** Low DHEA-S should prompt evaluation of adrenal function (morning cortisol, ACTH stimulation test if indicated). |
| **Interactions with other markers** | Interpret with **Cortisol** (adrenal co-production -- high cortisol + low DHEA-S = chronic stress pattern), **Total T + Free T** (DHEA-S is an androgen precursor), **Estradiol** (DHEA can aromatize to estrogen), **SHBG** (context for androgen bioavailability), **Fasting insulin** (insulin resistance affects adrenal androgen metabolism). |

### 11. LH (Luteinizing Hormone)

| Field | Content |
|-------|---------|
| **What it measures** | A gonadotropin produced by the anterior pituitary gland that stimulates Leydig cells in the testes to produce testosterone. LH is regulated by the hypothalamic-pituitary-gonadal (HPG) axis via GnRH pulses from the hypothalamus. |
| **Why it matters** | LH is the key diagnostic marker for distinguishing primary hypogonadism (testicular failure -- high LH) from secondary hypogonadism (pituitary/hypothalamic dysfunction -- low/normal LH). This distinction fundamentally changes treatment approach. LH also reflects the integrity of the HPG feedback loop. |
| **Standard reference range** | 1.8 -- 8.6 IU/L (LabCorp); 1.5 -- 9.3 IU/L (Quest) |
| **Optimal range** | **2.0 -- 6.0 IU/L.** For an active adult with normal testosterone: mid-range LH indicates a healthy, responsive HPG axis. LH at the upper end of normal with low-normal testosterone suggests the pituitary is compensating for declining testicular function (early primary hypogonadism). LH at the lower end with low testosterone suggests a central (pituitary/hypothalamic) problem. |
| **High values indicate** | Primary hypogonadism (testicular failure -- the pituitary is "screaming" at the testes to produce more testosterone): Klinefelter syndrome, testicular damage (trauma, torsion, radiation, chemotherapy), mumps orchitis, varicocele, aging testes. Also: post-GnRH agonist therapy rebound. |
| **Low values indicate** | Secondary/tertiary hypogonadism (pituitary or hypothalamic dysfunction): pituitary tumor/adenoma (especially prolactinoma), hyperprolactinemia, exogenous testosterone or anabolic steroid use (suppresses LH via negative feedback), opioid use, obesity, severe illness, Kallmann syndrome, excessive exercise or caloric restriction, head trauma. **Exogenous testosterone use will suppress LH to near-zero** -- this is the expected finding in TRT patients. |
| **Special considerations** | **Pulsatile secretion:** LH is released in pulses (every 60--90 minutes), so a single measurement is a snapshot. For borderline results, consider repeat testing or pooled samples. **Morning draw preferred** but less critical than testosterone. **On TRT/anabolics:** LH will be suppressed to <0.5 IU/L -- this is pharmacologically expected, not pathological. **Age effect:** LH tends to rise gradually with age as testicular function declines (compensated hypogonadism). **Medications:** Opioids, glucocorticoids, and high-dose estrogen suppress LH. Clomiphene and enclomiphene raise LH (used as alternatives to TRT). |
| **Interactions with other markers** | **Primary diagnostic triad: LH + FSH + Total T.** High LH + High FSH + Low T = primary hypogonadism. Low/normal LH + Low/normal FSH + Low T = secondary hypogonadism. Also check **Prolactin** (if LH/FSH are low, to rule out prolactinoma), **MRI pituitary** (if secondary hypogonadism confirmed), **Estradiol** (E2 exerts negative feedback on LH), **SHBG + Free T** (complete androgen picture). |

### 12. FSH (Follicle-Stimulating Hormone)

| Field | Content |
|-------|---------|
| **What it measures** | A gonadotropin produced by the anterior pituitary that stimulates Sertoli cells in the testes, supporting spermatogenesis (sperm production). In males, FSH is primarily a marker of spermatogenic function and testicular integrity, whereas LH drives testosterone production. |
| **Why it matters** | FSH is the primary marker of spermatogenic function and seminiferous tubule health. Elevated FSH in a male with low sperm count indicates irreversible testicular damage to the sperm-producing apparatus. Also helps distinguish primary vs. secondary hypogonadism alongside LH. Important for men concerned about fertility. |
| **Standard reference range** | 1.5 -- 12.4 IU/L (LabCorp); 1.4 -- 15.4 IU/L (Quest) |
| **Optimal range** | **1.5 -- 8.0 IU/L.** For an active adult male: mid-range FSH indicates healthy spermatogenic function. FSH above 10--12 IU/L with normal testosterone may indicate isolated seminiferous tubule damage (the testosterone-producing Leydig cells are still functioning but the sperm-producing machinery is compromised). |
| **High values indicate** | Primary testicular failure (seminiferous tubule damage): Klinefelter syndrome, Y-chromosome microdeletions, post-chemotherapy/radiation, mumps orchitis, varicocele, cryptorchidism, aging. FSH > 20 IU/L with azoospermia typically indicates irreversible spermatogenic failure. |
| **Low values indicate** | Secondary/tertiary hypogonadism (pituitary or hypothalamic dysfunction -- same causes as low LH): pituitary tumor, hyperprolactinemia, exogenous testosterone/anabolics (suppresses FSH), opioids, severe obesity, Kallmann syndrome, excessive exercise. **TRT suppresses FSH, which suppresses spermatogenesis** -- critical consideration for men wanting to preserve fertility. |
| **Special considerations** | **Fertility implications on TRT:** Exogenous testosterone suppresses FSH to near-zero, which drastically reduces or eliminates sperm production. For men who want to maintain fertility, alternatives like clomiphene/enclomiphene or HCG co-administration are used. **Less pulsatile than LH:** FSH has a longer half-life (~3--4 hours vs. ~20 minutes for LH), making single measurements more reliable. **Age effect:** FSH gradually rises with age as spermatogenic capacity declines. **Isolated FSH elevation:** High FSH with normal LH and normal testosterone = selective damage to seminiferous tubules (Sertoli cell only syndrome, post-chemo). |
| **Interactions with other markers** | **Interpret with LH + Total T** (primary vs. secondary hypogonadism triad). **Semen analysis** if fertility is a concern and FSH is elevated. Check **Prolactin** if both LH and FSH are low. **Inhibin B** (produced by Sertoli cells) inversely correlates with FSH and is a direct marker of spermatogenic function. **Karyotype/genetic testing** if FSH is very high with azoospermia. |

### 13. Prolactin

| Field | Content |
|-------|---------|
| **What it measures** | A peptide hormone produced by the anterior pituitary gland (lactotroph cells). In males, prolactin plays a role in immune regulation, osmoregulation, and modulates dopaminergic tone. Elevated prolactin suppresses GnRH, which in turn suppresses LH, FSH, and testosterone. |
| **Why it matters** | Hyperprolactinemia is an important and treatable cause of secondary hypogonadism, erectile dysfunction, and infertility in men. Significantly elevated prolactin (>50--100 ng/mL) raises concern for a pituitary prolactinoma. Even mildly elevated prolactin can contribute to low libido, erectile dysfunction, and mood disturbances. |
| **Standard reference range** | 4.0 -- 15.2 ng/mL (LabCorp); 2.1 -- 17.7 ng/mL (Quest) |
| **Optimal range** | **5 -- 12 ng/mL.** For an active adult male: low-to-mid range prolactin indicates healthy dopaminergic tone and no pituitary compression. Levels above 15--20 ng/mL warrant investigation, even if within the lab's "normal" range. Very low prolactin (<3 ng/mL) may indicate dopamine excess or pituitary insufficiency. |
| **High values indicate** | **Prolactinoma** (pituitary adenoma -- most common pituitary tumor): levels >200 ng/mL are virtually diagnostic of macroprolactinoma; levels 25--200 ng/mL may indicate microprolactinoma. **Medications** (most common cause of mild-moderate elevation): antipsychotics/neuroleptics, metoclopramide, SSRIs, high-dose estrogen, opioids. **Other:** Hypothyroidism (TRH stimulates prolactin), chest wall irritation, renal failure, cirrhosis, seizures, stress, nipple stimulation. **"Stalk effect":** Any pituitary mass (non-prolactinoma) compressing the stalk can mildly elevate prolactin (usually <100 ng/mL) by disrupting dopamine inhibition. |
| **Low values indicate** | Dopamine agonist therapy (cabergoline, bromocriptine), pituitary infarction (Sheehan's syndrome), excessive dopaminergic tone. Very low prolactin is generally not clinically significant in males unless accompanied by other pituitary deficiencies. |
| **Special considerations** | **Stress and venipuncture:** The act of blood drawing itself can transiently raise prolactin. For borderline elevations, repeat testing with the patient resting 15--20 minutes before the draw. **Macroprolactin:** Some assays detect macroprolactin (a biologically inactive, antibody-bound form) that falsely elevates results. If prolactin is elevated but the patient is asymptomatic, request macroprolactin testing. **"Hook effect":** Very large prolactinomas with extremely high prolactin (>10,000 ng/mL) can paradoxically show falsely normal or mildly elevated levels due to assay saturation. If a large pituitary mass is present with "normal" prolactin, request serial dilution. **Medications review:** Always review medications before attributing elevated prolactin to a prolactinoma -- drug-induced hyperprolactinemia is far more common. **Fasting:** Not strictly required but morning draw preferred for consistency. |
| **Interactions with other markers** | Interpret with **LH + FSH + Total T** (prolactin suppresses the entire HPG axis). If prolactin is elevated + low T + low LH/FSH = prolactin-mediated secondary hypogonadism. Check **TSH** (hypothyroidism causes mild hyperprolactinemia). **MRI pituitary** if prolactin >50 ng/mL or if <50 but LH/FSH are suppressed with no medication cause. **Visual field testing** if macroprolactinoma suspected (optic chiasm compression). **IGF-1** if pituitary mass found (screen for growth hormone co-secretion). |

## Quick Reference: Test Groupings for Interpretation

| Grouping | Tests | Why Together |
|----------|-------|-------------|
| **Thyroid full panel** | TSH + Free T3 + Free T4 + TPO Ab + TgAb | TSH alone misses conversion problems and subclinical autoimmune disease |
| **Androgen status** | Total T + Free T + SHBG | Total T without SHBG/Free T context is incomplete; high SHBG masks functional deficiency |
| **HPG axis integrity** | LH + FSH + Total T + Prolactin | Distinguishes primary vs. secondary hypogonadism and rules out prolactinoma |
| **Estrogen balance** | Estradiol (sensitive) + Total T + SHBG + Body fat % | E2 is driven by aromatization of T in adipose tissue; SHBG binds both T and E2 |
| **Adrenal androgens** | DHEA-S + Cortisol (AM) | Co-produced by adrenals; low DHEA-S with high cortisol = chronic stress pattern |
| **Metabolic-hormonal axis** | SHBG + Fasting insulin + Thyroid panel | Insulin resistance suppresses SHBG; thyroid status directly affects SHBG production |

## Pre-Test Protocol Checklist

- [ ] **Schedule draw for 7:00 -- 9:00 AM** (critical for testosterone accuracy in men under 45)
- [ ] **Fast 10--12 hours** before draw (water is fine)
- [ ] **Stop biotin supplements 3--7 days before** (interferes with thyroid assays and some immunoassays)
- [ ] **Avoid intense exercise 24 hours before** (transiently affects cortisol, testosterone, TSH)
- [ ] **Get normal sleep the night before** (sleep deprivation reduces testosterone 10--15%)
- [ ] **Note all current medications and supplements** for the ordering provider
- [ ] **Request sensitive/LC-MS/MS assay for estradiol** (standard immunoassay unreliable in males)
- [ ] **Request LC-MS/MS for testosterone** if available (more accurate than immunoassay)
- [ ] **Ensure calculated Free T** uses Vermeulen equation (avoid direct immunoassay for free T)
