## 1. CBC (Complete Blood Count)

The CBC is a foundational panel that evaluates the cellular components of blood: red blood cells (oxygen transport), white blood cells (immune function), and platelets (clotting). It is not a single test but a panel of 8+ individual markers.

---

### 1.1 White Blood Cell Count (WBC)

| Field | Content |
|-------|---------|
| **What it measures** | Total number of white blood cells (leukocytes) per microliter of blood. WBCs are the primary cells of the immune system. |
| **Why it matters** | Indicates immune system activity. Chronic low-grade elevation is associated with systemic inflammation, cardiovascular disease risk, and all-cause mortality even within the "normal" range. |
| **Standard reference range** | 3.4-10.8 x10^3/uL (Labcorp); 3.8-10.8 x10^3/uL (Quest) |
| **Optimal range** | 3.5-6.0 x10^3/uL. Functional medicine and longevity practitioners target the lower half of the standard range. Rupa Health optimal: 3.5-6.0. InsideTracker and research associate WBC >6.0 with increased inflammatory and cardiovascular risk. |
| **High values indicate** | Infection (bacterial, viral, fungal), inflammation, stress response, allergic reactions, leukemia or other blood cancers, autoimmune conditions, corticosteroid use, smoking |
| **Low values indicate** | Bone marrow suppression, autoimmune destruction of WBCs, viral infections (HIV, hepatitis), severe infections overwhelming immune capacity, chemotherapy, radiation, certain medications |
| **Special considerations** | Exercise can transiently elevate WBC for 24-48 hours. Stress and cortisol raise WBC. Smoking chronically elevates WBC. Draw in the morning in a rested state for most accurate baseline. Dehydration can falsely concentrate values. |
| **Interactions with other markers** | Interpret alongside differential (neutrophils, lymphocytes, monocytes, eosinophils, basophils), hs-CRP, ESR. Neutrophil-to-lymphocyte ratio (NLR) is a more refined inflammation marker. |

---

### 1.2 Red Blood Cell Count (RBC)

| Field | Content |
|-------|---------|
| **What it measures** | Number of red blood cells per microliter of blood. RBCs carry hemoglobin, which transports oxygen from lungs to tissues. |
| **Why it matters** | Reflects oxygen-carrying capacity and bone marrow production. Abnormalities indicate anemia, polycythemia, dehydration, or chronic disease. |
| **Standard reference range** | 4.50-5.90 x10^6/uL (males, Labcorp/NCBI) |
| **Optimal range** | 4.5-5.5 x10^6/uL. Functional medicine targets mid-range. Values at the high end may indicate dehydration or polycythemia. ODX functional range: 4.4-5.0. |
| **High values indicate** | Dehydration (most common cause of mild elevation), polycythemia vera, chronic hypoxia (COPD, sleep apnea, high altitude), testosterone/androgen use, EPO use, smoking |
| **Low values indicate** | Iron deficiency anemia, B12/folate deficiency, chronic disease anemia, blood loss, bone marrow disorders, chronic kidney disease (reduced EPO production), hemolysis |
| **Special considerations** | Altitude increases RBC production (acclimatization). Endurance exercise can cause "sports anemia" (dilutional, not true anemia). Hydration status significantly affects concentration. Testosterone therapy raises RBC count and should be monitored. |
| **Interactions with other markers** | Must interpret with hemoglobin, hematocrit, MCV, MCH, MCHC, reticulocyte count, iron studies (ferritin, TIBC), B12, folate. |

---

### 1.3 Hemoglobin (Hgb)

| Field | Content |
|-------|---------|
| **What it measures** | Concentration of hemoglobin protein in the blood (grams per deciliter). Hemoglobin binds oxygen in the lungs and delivers it to tissues. |
| **Why it matters** | The most direct measure of blood's oxygen-carrying capacity. Low hemoglobin = anemia = fatigue, poor exercise performance, cognitive impairment. High hemoglobin = increased blood viscosity = clotting risk. |
| **Standard reference range** | 13.0-17.7 g/dL (males, Labcorp); 14-18 g/dL (ACCP/traditional) |
| **Optimal range** | 14.0-16.0 g/dL. Functional medicine: 13.5-15.0 g/dL (ODX). For an active, fit male, 14.5-16.0 g/dL supports optimal oxygen delivery without excessive viscosity. InsideTracker personalizes to ~14.0-15.5 for active males. |
| **High values indicate** | Dehydration, polycythemia vera, chronic hypoxia, smoking, testosterone use, EPO doping, living at high altitude |
| **Low values indicate** | Iron deficiency anemia, B12/folate deficiency, chronic disease, blood loss (GI bleeds, heavy exercise-induced hemolysis), kidney disease, thalassemia, bone marrow failure |
| **Special considerations** | Endurance athletes may have lower hemoglobin due to plasma volume expansion ("sports anemia" -- not pathological). Fasting state preferred. Dehydration concentrates hemoglobin artificially. High-altitude living naturally raises hemoglobin. |
| **Interactions with other markers** | Hematocrit, RBC, MCV, ferritin, iron, TIBC, B12, folate, reticulocyte count, HbA1c (shares the same molecule). |

---

### 1.4 Hematocrit (Hct)

| Field | Content |
|-------|---------|
| **What it measures** | Percentage of blood volume occupied by red blood cells. Essentially the ratio of RBC volume to total blood volume. |
| **Why it matters** | Reflects blood's oxygen-carrying capacity and viscosity. Important for cardiovascular risk -- excessively high hematocrit thickens blood and increases stroke/clot risk. |
| **Standard reference range** | 37.5-51.0% (males, Labcorp); 40-52% (NCBI) |
| **Optimal range** | 40-48%. Functional medicine: 40-50% (ODX). For a fit, active male: 42-48% balances oxygen delivery with appropriate viscosity. Values consistently >50% warrant investigation. |
| **High values indicate** | Dehydration (most common), polycythemia vera, chronic hypoxia, testosterone therapy, EPO use, smoking, sleep apnea |
| **Low values indicate** | Anemia (any cause), overhydration, blood loss, chronic disease, bone marrow disorders, nutritional deficiencies |
| **Special considerations** | Hematocrit is highly sensitive to hydration status -- always interpret in context. Men on TRT should monitor hematocrit closely (>52-54% is a common threshold for dose adjustment or therapeutic phlebotomy). Endurance athletes often have lower hematocrit due to expanded plasma volume. |
| **Interactions with other markers** | Hemoglobin (Hct is roughly 3x Hgb), RBC count, MCV, ferritin, EPO levels, testosterone levels. |

---

### 1.5 Platelets (PLT)

| Field | Content |
|-------|---------|
| **What it measures** | Number of platelets (thrombocytes) per microliter of blood. Platelets are cell fragments produced by bone marrow megakaryocytes that form clots to stop bleeding. |
| **Why it matters** | Too few platelets = bleeding risk. Too many = clotting risk (stroke, DVT, PE). Platelet count also reflects bone marrow health and can signal inflammation or chronic disease. |
| **Standard reference range** | 150-400 x10^3/uL (Labcorp/Quest); 150-350 (ACCP) |
| **Optimal range** | 200-300 x10^3/uL. Functional medicine targets the middle of the range. Rupa Health optimal: 200-300. Values at extremes of "normal" warrant further evaluation. |
| **High values indicate** | Reactive thrombocytosis (infection, inflammation, iron deficiency, post-surgery), essential thrombocythemia, other myeloproliferative disorders, chronic inflammatory conditions |
| **Low values indicate** | Immune thrombocytopenic purpura (ITP), bone marrow failure, viral infections, liver disease/cirrhosis (splenic sequestration), medications (heparin, chemotherapy), alcohol abuse, B12/folate deficiency |
| **Special considerations** | Platelets can clump in EDTA tubes causing falsely low counts (pseudothrombocytopenia). Intense exercise can transiently elevate platelets. Aspirin and NSAIDs affect platelet function but not count. Recheck unexpected results before investigating. |
| **Interactions with other markers** | MPV (mean platelet volume), WBC, fibrinogen, PT/INR, D-dimer, liver function tests. |

---

### 1.6 Mean Corpuscular Volume (MCV)

| Field | Content |
|-------|---------|
| **What it measures** | Average volume (size) of a single red blood cell, measured in femtoliters (fL). Calculated from hematocrit and RBC count. |
| **Why it matters** | Key for classifying anemia by type: microcytic (small RBCs), normocytic (normal-sized), or macrocytic (large RBCs). Directly guides investigation into the cause of anemia. |
| **Standard reference range** | 80-100 fL (universal) |
| **Optimal range** | 85-92 fL. Functional medicine targets the tighter mid-range. ODX/functional: 85-92 fL. Values at the edges of normal suggest early nutrient depletion. |
| **High values indicate** | B12 deficiency, folate deficiency, alcoholism, liver disease, hypothyroidism, myelodysplastic syndromes, certain medications (methotrexate, hydroxyurea), reticulocytosis |
| **Low values indicate** | Iron deficiency (most common), thalassemia trait, chronic disease anemia, lead poisoning, sideroblastic anemia |
| **Special considerations** | MCV rises normally with age. Alcohol consumption raises MCV even without frank deficiency. Combined iron + B12 deficiency can produce a normal MCV (the two effects cancel out), masking dual deficiency. |
| **Interactions with other markers** | RDW (red cell distribution width), ferritin, iron, TIBC, B12, folate, reticulocyte count, peripheral blood smear. |

---

### 1.7 Mean Corpuscular Hemoglobin (MCH)

| Field | Content |
|-------|---------|
| **What it measures** | Average amount of hemoglobin per red blood cell, measured in picograms (pg). Calculated from hemoglobin and RBC count. |
| **Why it matters** | Reflects how well each red blood cell is loaded with hemoglobin. Parallels MCV -- low MCH typically accompanies microcytic anemia, high MCH accompanies macrocytic anemia. |
| **Standard reference range** | 26-34 pg/cell (ACCP); 27-33 pg (most labs) |
| **Optimal range** | 27.7-32.0 pg. Functional medicine: 27.7-32.0 pg (ODX). |
| **High values indicate** | Macrocytic anemia (B12/folate deficiency), liver disease, hypothyroidism |
| **Low values indicate** | Iron deficiency, thalassemia, chronic disease anemia |
| **Special considerations** | MCH generally tracks with MCV. Rarely clinically useful independent of MCV. Most significant when discordant with MCV. |
| **Interactions with other markers** | MCV, MCHC, hemoglobin, RBC, ferritin, B12, folate. |

---

### 1.8 Mean Corpuscular Hemoglobin Concentration (MCHC)

| Field | Content |
|-------|---------|
| **What it measures** | Average concentration of hemoglobin within a given volume of red blood cells (g/dL). Calculated from hemoglobin and hematocrit. |
| **Why it matters** | Indicates whether RBCs are appropriately saturated with hemoglobin. Low MCHC = hypochromic (pale) cells. High MCHC = hyperchromic cells (rare, seen in spherocytosis). |
| **Standard reference range** | 33-37 g/dL (ACCP); 30-35 g/dL (NCBI) |
| **Optimal range** | 32-36 g/dL. Functional medicine: 32-36 g/dL (ODX). |
| **High values indicate** | Hereditary spherocytosis, severe dehydration, cold agglutinin disease (artifact), sickle cell disease |
| **Low values indicate** | Iron deficiency anemia, thalassemia, chronic disease |
| **Special considerations** | MCHC is relatively stable and resistant to change. A truly elevated MCHC is uncommon and should prompt investigation for spherocytosis or lab artifact. |
| **Interactions with other markers** | MCV, MCH, hemoglobin, hematocrit, reticulocyte count, peripheral smear. |

## 2. CMP (Comprehensive Metabolic Panel)

The CMP is a panel of 14 tests that evaluates glucose metabolism, kidney function, liver function, and electrolyte/fluid balance.

---

### 2.1 Glucose (Fasting)

| Field | Content |
|-------|---------|
| **What it measures** | Concentration of glucose (blood sugar) in the blood after an overnight fast of 8-12 hours. |
| **Why it matters** | The primary fuel source for cells. Fasting glucose reflects baseline insulin sensitivity and glucose regulation. Elevated fasting glucose is the earliest detectable sign of insulin resistance, metabolic syndrome, and progression toward type 2 diabetes. |
| **Standard reference range** | 65-99 mg/dL (Quest/Labcorp); 70-110 mg/dL (older references) |
| **Optimal range** | 72-85 mg/dL. Peter Attia targets average glucose around 85 mg/dL (corresponding to HbA1c ~4.6%). Mark Hyman and functional medicine practitioners recommend 70-85 mg/dL. Rupa Health optimal: 80-89 mg/dL. Multiple functional sources converge on the mid-70s to mid-80s as ideal. |
| **High values indicate** | Insulin resistance, prediabetes (100-125 mg/dL), diabetes (126+ mg/dL), stress/cortisol elevation, Cushing's syndrome, pancreatitis, medications (steroids, thiazides), acute illness |
| **Low values indicate** | Reactive hypoglycemia, insulinoma, excessive insulin/medication dosing, adrenal insufficiency, liver failure, severe infection/sepsis, prolonged fasting, excessive alcohol |
| **Special considerations** | **Must be fasting 8-12 hours.** Caffeine can raise glucose. Acute stress/cortisol elevates glucose. Time of day matters -- cortisol-driven dawn phenomenon can raise morning glucose. Some people show higher fasting glucose on very low-carb diets ("physiological insulin resistance"). Intense exercise the prior day can affect levels. |
| **Interactions with other markers** | HbA1c (3-month average), fasting insulin (together calculate HOMA-IR), C-peptide, triglycerides, triglyceride/HDL ratio (insulin resistance proxy). |

---

### 2.2 Blood Urea Nitrogen (BUN)

| Field | Content |
|-------|---------|
| **What it measures** | Concentration of urea nitrogen in the blood. Urea is a waste product of protein metabolism produced by the liver and excreted by the kidneys. |
| **Why it matters** | Reflects kidney function and hydration status. Also influenced by protein intake and liver function. Part of the kidney function assessment alongside creatinine and eGFR. |
| **Standard reference range** | 6-24 mg/dL (Quest); 8-21 mg/dL (NCBI); 7-20 mg/dL (Labcorp) |
| **Optimal range** | 10-20 mg/dL. Functional medicine: 13-18 mg/dL (ODX). For an active male eating adequate protein (0.7-1.0 g/lb), BUN in the 13-20 range is normal and expected. |
| **High values indicate** | Dehydration (most common mild elevation), high protein diet, GI bleeding, kidney disease/failure, heart failure (reduced kidney perfusion), urinary obstruction, catabolic states, medications (corticosteroids) |
| **Low values indicate** | Low protein intake, severe liver disease (impaired urea production), overhydration, malnutrition, celiac disease |
| **Special considerations** | High protein intake (common in active individuals) physiologically raises BUN -- this is not pathological. Hydration status significantly affects BUN. Interpret BUN/creatinine ratio: normal is 10:1 to 20:1. Ratio >20:1 suggests pre-renal cause (dehydration, GI bleed). |
| **Interactions with other markers** | Creatinine, eGFR, BUN/creatinine ratio, protein intake assessment, albumin, liver function tests. |

---

### 2.3 Creatinine

| Field | Content |
|-------|---------|
| **What it measures** | Blood concentration of creatinine, a waste product of muscle metabolism (breakdown of creatine phosphate). Produced at a relatively constant rate based on muscle mass and excreted by kidneys. |
| **Why it matters** | The most commonly used marker for estimating kidney (glomerular) filtration. Because production is proportional to muscle mass, it is a useful but imperfect marker of kidney function. |
| **Standard reference range** | 0.7-1.3 mg/dL (males, Quest/Labcorp); 0.6-1.2 mg/dL (ACCP) |
| **Optimal range** | 0.85-1.10 mg/dL (males). Functional medicine (ODX): 0.85-1.1 for males. For a muscular male, values up to 1.2 may be physiologically normal without indicating kidney impairment. Creatine supplementation raises creatinine. |
| **High values indicate** | Kidney disease/impaired filtration (most important cause), dehydration, high muscle mass (physiological), creatine supplementation, high meat intake, rhabdomyolysis, medications (ACE inhibitors, NSAIDs can raise it) |
| **Low values indicate** | Low muscle mass, sarcopenia, malnutrition, severe liver disease, pregnancy |
| **Special considerations** | **Creatine supplementation raises creatinine** -- inform your physician if supplementing. Muscular individuals routinely run higher creatinine without kidney disease. Intense exercise can transiently raise creatinine. Cystatin C is a better kidney function marker for muscular individuals as it is not affected by muscle mass. |
| **Interactions with other markers** | eGFR (calculated from creatinine, age, sex), BUN, BUN/creatinine ratio, cystatin C, urine albumin-to-creatinine ratio (UACR). |

---

### 2.4 Estimated Glomerular Filtration Rate (eGFR)

| Field | Content |
|-------|---------|
| **What it measures** | A calculation (not a direct measurement) estimating how much blood the kidneys filter per minute, derived from serum creatinine, age, sex, and (in some formulas) race. Reported in mL/min/1.73m^2. |
| **Why it matters** | The primary clinical metric for staging chronic kidney disease (CKD). The gold standard for assessing overall kidney filtration capacity. |
| **Standard reference range** | >60 mL/min/1.73m^2 (normal); >90 = normal kidney function; 60-89 = mildly decreased; <60 = CKD stage 3+ |
| **Optimal range** | >90 mL/min/1.73m^2, ideally >100. Note: muscular individuals with higher creatinine may show artificially lower eGFR. If eGFR seems low but creatinine is elevated due to muscle mass, request cystatin C-based eGFR for a more accurate assessment. |
| **High values indicate** | Normal kidney function (this is good). Very high eGFR (>120-130) in the setting of diabetes may indicate glomerular hyperfiltration, an early sign of diabetic nephropathy. |
| **Low values indicate** | Chronic kidney disease (staged by eGFR level), acute kidney injury, dehydration, nephrotoxic medications, obstructive uropathy |
| **Special considerations** | The CKD-EPI 2021 equation (race-free) is now the standard formula. eGFR is less accurate at extremes of body size and muscle mass. For a muscular male, eGFR may underestimate true kidney function. Cystatin C-based eGFR is recommended if discrepancy suspected. |
| **Interactions with other markers** | Creatinine, cystatin C, BUN, urine albumin, blood pressure, glucose/HbA1c (diabetic nephropathy). |

---

### 2.5 Sodium (Na)

| Field | Content |
|-------|---------|
| **What it measures** | Serum concentration of sodium, the primary extracellular cation. Regulated by the kidneys, ADH (antidiuretic hormone), and aldosterone. |
| **Why it matters** | Critical for fluid balance, nerve impulse transmission, and muscle contraction. Abnormalities can be life-threatening. Reflects the balance between water and sodium rather than total body sodium alone. |
| **Standard reference range** | 136-145 mmol/L (Quest); 134-144 mmol/L (Labcorp) |
| **Optimal range** | 138-142 mmol/L. Functional medicine (ODX): 134-140. Rupa Health optimal: 138-142. Mid-range is ideal. |
| **High values indicate** | Dehydration (most common), diabetes insipidus, excess sodium intake, Cushing's syndrome, hyperaldosteronism, inadequate water intake |
| **Low values indicate** | Overhydration/hyponatremia (very common in endurance athletes), SIADH, heart failure, cirrhosis, kidney disease, diuretics, adrenal insufficiency, hypothyroidism, excessive sweating with water-only replacement |
| **Special considerations** | Endurance athletes are at risk for exercise-associated hyponatremia from excessive water intake without electrolytes. Medications (diuretics, SSRIs, carbamazepine) can lower sodium. Pseudohyponatremia can occur with very high lipids or proteins. |
| **Interactions with other markers** | Potassium, chloride, CO2 (anion gap calculation), osmolality, BUN/creatinine (kidney function), glucose (corrected sodium in hyperglycemia). |

---

### 2.6 Potassium (K)

| Field | Content |
|-------|---------|
| **What it measures** | Serum concentration of potassium, the primary intracellular cation. Only ~2% of total body potassium is in the serum, so small changes in serum K reflect large shifts in total body potassium. |
| **Why it matters** | Critical for cardiac rhythm, muscle contraction, and nerve function. Both hypo- and hyperkalemia can cause fatal cardiac arrhythmias. Tightly regulated. |
| **Standard reference range** | 3.5-5.2 mmol/L (Labcorp); 3.5-5.0 mmol/L (NCBI/ACCP) |
| **Optimal range** | 4.0-5.0 mmol/L. Functional medicine (ODX): 4.5-5.1. Rupa Health optimal: 4.0-5.0. Mid-to-upper range is associated with better cardiovascular outcomes. |
| **High values indicate** | **Hemolysis during blood draw (most common cause of false elevation)**, kidney disease/failure, acidosis, ACE inhibitors/ARBs, potassium-sparing diuretics, Addison's disease, rhabdomyolysis, excessive supplementation |
| **Low values indicate** | Diuretics (thiazides, loop diuretics), vomiting/diarrhea, poor dietary intake, alkalosis, insulin administration, hyperaldosteronism, magnesium deficiency (prevents potassium retention) |
| **Special considerations** | **Hemolyzed specimens are the most common cause of falsely elevated potassium** -- always confirm unexpected highs with a repeat draw. Intense exercise can transiently raise serum K. Fist clenching during blood draw can falsely elevate K. Magnesium must be repleted before potassium can be effectively corrected. |
| **Interactions with other markers** | Sodium, magnesium, bicarbonate/CO2, kidney function (creatinine, eGFR), aldosterone, ECG findings. |

---

### 2.7 Chloride (Cl)

| Field | Content |
|-------|---------|
| **What it measures** | Serum concentration of chloride, the primary extracellular anion. Works in tandem with sodium to maintain fluid balance and acid-base equilibrium. |
| **Why it matters** | Part of the acid-base system. Used to calculate the anion gap, which helps diagnose metabolic acidosis causes. Generally moves in parallel with sodium. |
| **Standard reference range** | 96-106 mmol/L (Quest/Labcorp); 95-105 mmol/L (NCBI) |
| **Optimal range** | 100-106 mmol/L. Functional medicine (ODX): 100-106. |
| **High values indicate** | Dehydration, renal tubular acidosis, excessive saline infusion, respiratory alkalosis, hyperparathyroidism |
| **Low values indicate** | Vomiting (loss of HCl), metabolic alkalosis, SIADH, Addison's disease, diuretics, overhydration |
| **Special considerations** | Chloride is most useful when interpreted with sodium and CO2 to calculate the anion gap. Rarely abnormal in isolation. |
| **Interactions with other markers** | Sodium, CO2/bicarbonate, anion gap = Na - (Cl + CO2). Normal anion gap: 8-12 mEq/L. |

---

### 2.8 Carbon Dioxide (CO2 / Bicarbonate)

| Field | Content |
|-------|---------|
| **What it measures** | Total CO2 content of serum, which primarily reflects bicarbonate (HCO3-) levels. Bicarbonate is the body's main acid-base buffer. |
| **Why it matters** | Reflects acid-base status. Low CO2 = metabolic acidosis. High CO2 = metabolic alkalosis. Important for diagnosing acid-base disturbances. |
| **Standard reference range** | 20-29 mmol/L (Quest/Labcorp); 22-26 mEq/L (ACCP) |
| **Optimal range** | 23-27 mmol/L. Functional medicine (ODX): 22-27. Rupa Health optimal: 23-30. |
| **High values indicate** | Metabolic alkalosis (vomiting, diuretics), chronic respiratory acidosis (COPD compensating), excessive antacid use |
| **Low values indicate** | Metabolic acidosis (diabetic ketoacidosis, lactic acidosis, renal failure, diarrhea), respiratory alkalosis compensation, chronic kidney disease |
| **Special considerations** | Specimens should be processed quickly; CO2 escapes from blood exposed to air, causing falsely low results. Intense exercise (lactic acid production) can lower CO2 acutely. |
| **Interactions with other markers** | Sodium, chloride, potassium (anion gap calculation), BUN/creatinine (kidney function), glucose (DKA), lactate. |

---

### 2.9 Calcium (Ca)

| Field | Content |
|-------|---------|
| **What it measures** | Total serum calcium concentration. About 40% is bound to albumin, 10% to other anions, and 50% is free/ionized (the biologically active form). |
| **Why it matters** | Essential for bone health, muscle contraction, nerve signaling, blood clotting, and cardiac rhythm. Tightly regulated by parathyroid hormone (PTH), vitamin D, and calcitonin. |
| **Standard reference range** | 8.5-10.5 mg/dL (Quest/Labcorp); 8.5-10.2 mg/dL (NCBI) |
| **Optimal range** | 9.2-10.0 mg/dL. Functional medicine (ODX): 9.2-10.1. Rupa Health flags >9.9 as above optimal. Mid-range is ideal -- consistently elevated calcium (even within range) warrants PTH check. |
| **High values indicate** | Primary hyperparathyroidism (most common outpatient cause), malignancy (most common inpatient cause), excess vitamin D supplementation, thiazide diuretics, granulomatous disease, immobilization, hyperthyroidism |
| **Low values indicate** | Hypoparathyroidism, vitamin D deficiency, kidney disease, magnesium deficiency, pancreatitis, malabsorption, low albumin (correct for albumin: add 0.8 mg/dL for each 1 g/dL albumin below 4.0) |
| **Special considerations** | **Always correct calcium for albumin** if albumin is low. Ionized calcium is more accurate but requires special handling. Vitamin D supplementation affects calcium levels. Calcium >10.5 with elevated PTH = primary hyperparathyroidism until proven otherwise. |
| **Interactions with other markers** | Albumin (for corrected calcium), PTH, vitamin D (25-OH), phosphorus, magnesium, alkaline phosphatase. |

---

### 2.10 Albumin

| Field | Content |
|-------|---------|
| **What it measures** | Serum concentration of albumin, the most abundant protein in the blood. Produced by the liver. Functions as a transport protein and maintains oncotic pressure (keeps fluid in blood vessels). |
| **Why it matters** | A marker of nutritional status, liver synthetic function, and inflammation. Low albumin is one of the strongest predictors of morbidity and mortality in hospitalized patients. For longevity, higher albumin within range is associated with better outcomes. |
| **Standard reference range** | 3.5-5.5 g/dL (Quest/Labcorp); 3.5-5.0 g/dL (NCBI) |
| **Optimal range** | 4.2-5.0 g/dL. Functional medicine (ODX): 4.0-5.0. For longevity optimization, albumin >4.0 is a strong positive signal. Peter Attia and longevity researchers note albumin <3.5 carries significant mortality risk, and values >4.5 are associated with the best outcomes. |
| **High values indicate** | Dehydration (most common cause of mild elevation). Truly elevated albumin is rare. |
| **Low values indicate** | Malnutrition/inadequate protein intake, liver disease (cirrhosis, hepatitis), chronic inflammation (albumin is a negative acute phase reactant -- drops during illness), nephrotic syndrome (urinary protein loss), malabsorption, chronic disease |
| **Special considerations** | Albumin drops during acute illness and inflammation (as a negative acute phase reactant). Not reliable during acute illness. Reflects protein status over weeks, not days. Adequate protein intake (critical for a active male) supports albumin levels. |
| **Interactions with other markers** | Total protein, globulin (calculated: total protein - albumin), A/G ratio, liver enzymes (AST, ALT, ALP), calcium (corrected calcium), prealbumin (more sensitive short-term nutritional marker). |

---

### 2.11 Total Protein

| Field | Content |
|-------|---------|
| **What it measures** | Total concentration of all proteins in the serum, which is predominantly albumin (~60%) and globulins (~40%, including immunoglobulins, transport proteins, clotting factors). |
| **Why it matters** | Provides a broad assessment of protein status. The albumin-to-globulin (A/G) ratio is clinically useful for identifying chronic inflammation, liver disease, or immune disorders. |
| **Standard reference range** | 6.0-8.3 g/dL (Quest/Labcorp); 6.0-8.0 g/dL (NCBI) |
| **Optimal range** | 6.9-7.4 g/dL. Functional medicine (ODX): 6.9-7.4. Rupa Health flags >6.9 as above optimal threshold. |
| **High values indicate** | Dehydration, chronic inflammation, chronic infections (HIV, hepatitis), multiple myeloma, autoimmune disease |
| **Low values indicate** | Malnutrition, liver disease, nephrotic syndrome, malabsorption, immunodeficiency |
| **Special considerations** | Total protein alone is not very informative -- the breakdown into albumin and globulin (and the A/G ratio) is what matters. A/G ratio normally ~1.1-2.5. Low A/G suggests chronic inflammation or liver disease. |
| **Interactions with other markers** | Albumin, globulin, A/G ratio, liver enzymes, immunoglobulin levels (IgG, IgA, IgM if further workup needed). |

---

### 2.12 Alkaline Phosphatase (ALP)

| Field | Content |
|-------|---------|
| **What it measures** | Serum level of alkaline phosphatase, an enzyme found in liver, bone, kidneys, and intestines. Most circulating ALP comes from liver and bone. |
| **Why it matters** | Elevated ALP can indicate liver disease (especially biliary obstruction) or bone disorders. In the context of a CMP, it is primarily a liver/biliary marker. |
| **Standard reference range** | 44-147 IU/L (Quest/Labcorp); 50-100 U/L (NCBI) |
| **Optimal range** | 50-90 IU/L. Functional medicine (ODX): 44-90. Rupa Health optimal: 48-51 (very narrow). Values persistently >100 warrant investigation even if within "standard" range. |
| **High values indicate** | Biliary obstruction (gallstones, tumors), cholestatic liver disease, bone disorders (Paget's disease, fracture healing, bone metastases), hyperparathyroidism, vitamin D deficiency (compensatory bone turnover), pregnancy, growth in adolescents |
| **Low values indicate** | Zinc deficiency, malnutrition, hypothyroidism, pernicious anemia, Wilson's disease, celiac disease |
| **Special considerations** | ALP is normally higher in growing children/adolescents (bone growth). When ALP is elevated, GGT helps differentiate liver vs. bone source: if GGT is also elevated, the source is liver; if GGT is normal, the source is likely bone. |
| **Interactions with other markers** | GGT (liver vs. bone differentiation), bilirubin, ALT, AST, calcium, vitamin D, PTH. |

---

### 2.13 Alanine Aminotransferase (ALT / SGPT)

| Field | Content |
|-------|---------|
| **What it measures** | Serum level of ALT, an enzyme predominantly found in the liver (with small amounts in kidneys and muscle). More specific to the liver than AST. |
| **Why it matters** | The most liver-specific of the standard liver enzymes. Elevation indicates hepatocyte (liver cell) damage or inflammation. Key marker for fatty liver disease (NAFLD/MASLD), which affects ~25% of adults and is strongly linked to metabolic syndrome. |
| **Standard reference range** | 7-56 IU/L (Quest/Labcorp); 5-30 U/L (NCBI) |
| **Optimal range** | <30 IU/L for males. Functional medicine: <30 (ODX). Rupa Health optimal: 7-29. The American College of Gastroenterology recommends upper limit of 33 IU/L for males. Values consistently >25-30 warrant evaluation for fatty liver even if "within range." |
| **High values indicate** | Non-alcoholic fatty liver disease (NAFLD/MASLD -- the most common cause), alcoholic liver disease, viral hepatitis, drug-induced liver injury (acetaminophen, statins, supplements), autoimmune hepatitis, celiac disease, strenuous exercise (transient) |
| **Low values indicate** | Generally not clinically significant. Very low ALT may reflect low muscle mass or B6 deficiency (ALT requires B6 as a cofactor). |
| **Special considerations** | **Intense exercise (especially eccentric/resistance training) can elevate ALT for 24-72 hours.** Avoid heavy exercise 48-72 hours before blood draw for accurate liver assessment. Acetaminophen, statins, and many supplements (especially kava, green tea extract, high-dose niacin) can raise ALT. |
| **Interactions with other markers** | AST (AST/ALT ratio >2 suggests alcoholic liver disease; ratio <1 suggests NAFLD), ALP, GGT, bilirubin, albumin, fasting glucose/insulin (metabolic syndrome and NAFLD link). |

---

### 2.14 Aspartate Aminotransferase (AST / SGOT)

| Field | Content |
|-------|---------|
| **What it measures** | Serum level of AST, an enzyme found in liver, heart, skeletal muscle, kidneys, brain, and RBCs. Less liver-specific than ALT because it is present in many tissues. |
| **Why it matters** | Elevated AST indicates tissue damage, most commonly liver or muscle. The AST/ALT ratio helps differentiate causes of liver disease. Also relevant for monitoring statin-related muscle effects. |
| **Standard reference range** | 10-40 IU/L (Quest/Labcorp); 5-30 U/L (NCBI) |
| **Optimal range** | <30 IU/L for males. Functional medicine: <30 (ODX). Rupa Health optimal: 15-18 (very narrow). Practically, <30 with an AST/ALT ratio ~1.0 is ideal. |
| **High values indicate** | Liver disease (all types), muscle damage (rhabdomyolysis, intense exercise, statin myopathy), heart damage (myocardial infarction), hemolysis, thyroid disease |
| **Low values indicate** | Generally not clinically significant. May reflect B6 deficiency or uremia. |
| **Special considerations** | **AST is more sensitive to exercise-induced muscle damage than ALT.** Resistance training can elevate AST significantly for 24-72 hours. The AST/ALT (De Ritis) ratio is clinically important: ratio >2 suggests alcoholic liver disease; ratio ~1 suggests NAFLD; ratio <1 with both mildly elevated suggests simple steatosis. |
| **Interactions with other markers** | ALT (AST/ALT ratio), ALP, GGT, bilirubin, CK (creatine kinase -- distinguishes muscle vs. liver source), LDH, albumin. |

---

### 2.15 Total Bilirubin

| Field | Content |
|-------|---------|
| **What it measures** | Total concentration of bilirubin in the blood. Bilirubin is a yellow pigment produced from the breakdown of hemoglobin in old red blood cells. Processed by the liver and excreted in bile. |
| **Why it matters** | Reflects liver function, bile duct patency, and red blood cell turnover. Elevated bilirubin causes jaundice (yellowing of skin and eyes). Interestingly, mildly elevated bilirubin (as in Gilbert's syndrome) has antioxidant properties and is associated with reduced cardiovascular disease risk. |
| **Standard reference range** | 0.1-1.2 mg/dL (Quest/Labcorp); 0.1-1.0 mg/dL (some labs) |
| **Optimal range** | 0.5-1.2 mg/dL. Functional medicine (ODX): 0.1-1.2. Rupa Health optimal: 0.5-0.7. Mildly elevated bilirubin (1.0-1.5) in the absence of liver disease is actually associated with cardiovascular protection and may reflect Gilbert's syndrome (benign genetic variant affecting ~5-10% of the population). |
| **High values indicate** | Liver disease (hepatitis, cirrhosis), bile duct obstruction (gallstones, tumors), hemolytic anemia (excessive RBC breakdown), Gilbert's syndrome (benign, genetic), neonatal jaundice, medications |
| **Low values indicate** | Generally not clinically significant. |
| **Special considerations** | Gilbert's syndrome (UGT1A1 mutation) causes intermittent mild unconjugated hyperbilirubinemia, especially during fasting, stress, or illness. This is benign and may actually be protective. Fasting can raise bilirubin. Direct (conjugated) vs. indirect (unconjugated) bilirubin fractionation helps determine the cause of elevation. |
| **Interactions with other markers** | ALT, AST, ALP, GGT (liver disease pattern), hemoglobin/hematocrit/reticulocyte count (hemolysis), LDH, haptoglobin. |

## 3. Lipid Panel

The lipid panel measures blood fats (lipids) that are central to cardiovascular disease risk assessment. Note: Peter Attia and many longevity practitioners consider ApoB a superior marker to standard LDL-C, but the standard lipid panel remains the most commonly ordered test.

---

### 3.1 Total Cholesterol

| Field | Content |
|-------|---------|
| **What it measures** | Total concentration of all cholesterol carried in all lipoprotein particles (HDL + LDL + VLDL + other). |
| **Why it matters** | A crude overall measure of cholesterol. Less useful than individual fractions (LDL, HDL) for risk assessment. Total cholesterol alone is a poor predictor of cardiovascular risk because it does not distinguish between protective HDL and atherogenic LDL. |
| **Standard reference range** | <200 mg/dL desirable; 200-239 borderline high; >=240 high (Quest/Labcorp/ATP III) |
| **Optimal range** | 150-200 mg/dL for longevity optimization. Functional medicine (ODX): 150-220. The composition matters far more than the total number. A total cholesterol of 220 with high HDL and low triglycerides may be healthier than 180 with low HDL and high triglycerides. Peter Attia focuses on ApoB rather than total cholesterol. |
| **High values indicate** | Familial hypercholesterolemia, dietary factors, hypothyroidism, nephrotic syndrome, liver disease, medications (some), metabolic syndrome |
| **Low values indicate** | Hyperthyroidism, liver failure, malnutrition, malabsorption, chronic illness, some genetic conditions. Very low total cholesterol (<120) is associated with increased mortality risk. |
| **Special considerations** | Non-fasting lipid panels are now accepted for total cholesterol, HDL, and LDL-C in many guidelines. However, fasting is still preferred for triglycerides. Total cholesterol can be high solely because of high HDL (which is protective). |
| **Interactions with other markers** | HDL, LDL, triglycerides, total cholesterol/HDL ratio, non-HDL cholesterol (total - HDL), ApoB, Lp(a). |

---

### 3.2 LDL Cholesterol (LDL-C)

| Field | Content |
|-------|---------|
| **What it measures** | Cholesterol carried in low-density lipoprotein particles. Usually calculated (Friedewald equation: LDL = Total Chol - HDL - Trig/5) rather than directly measured. Direct LDL measurement is more accurate when triglycerides are >400 or <100. |
| **Why it matters** | LDL is the primary atherogenic lipoprotein -- it deposits cholesterol in arterial walls, driving plaque formation. Reducing LDL-C is the cornerstone of cardiovascular disease prevention. However, LDL-C is an imperfect measure because it counts cholesterol content, not particle number (ApoB is better). |
| **Standard reference range** | <100 mg/dL optimal; 100-129 near optimal; 130-159 borderline high; 160-189 high; >=190 very high (Quest/Labcorp/ATP III) |
| **Optimal range** | <100 mg/dL, ideally <80 mg/dL for longevity. Peter Attia's approach targets ApoB <60 mg/dL (which generally corresponds to LDL-C in the 60-80 range). For primary prevention In an active adult male optimizing for longevity, <100 mg/dL is a minimum target and <80 is ideal. Attia argues for aggressive lipid management based on cumulative LDL exposure over a lifetime. |
| **High values indicate** | Familial hypercholesterolemia, high saturated fat diet, hypothyroidism, nephrotic syndrome, obstructive liver disease, diabetes, metabolic syndrome, certain medications |
| **Low values indicate** | Hyperthyroidism, liver disease, malnutrition, genetic hypobetalipoproteinemia, statin therapy |
| **Special considerations** | Calculated LDL-C (Friedewald) is inaccurate when triglycerides >400 mg/dL or very low (<40 mg/dL). The Martin-Hopkins or direct LDL measurement is more accurate in these cases. LDL-C does not account for particle number -- a person can have "normal" LDL-C but high LDL particle count (discordance). ApoB is the better metric. |
| **Interactions with other markers** | ApoB (particle count), Lp(a), triglycerides (LDL calculation accuracy), HDL, total cholesterol, hs-CRP, coronary artery calcium (CAC) score. |

---

### 3.3 HDL Cholesterol (HDL-C)

| Field | Content |
|-------|---------|
| **What it measures** | Cholesterol carried in high-density lipoprotein particles. HDL participates in reverse cholesterol transport -- removing cholesterol from arterial walls and returning it to the liver for excretion. |
| **Why it matters** | Higher HDL is generally associated with lower cardiovascular risk. However, very high HDL (>90-100) may not confer additional protection and in some genetic contexts may paradoxically increase risk. HDL function (cholesterol efflux capacity) matters more than HDL-C concentration alone. |
| **Standard reference range** | >=40 mg/dL for males (Quest/Labcorp); >=60 mg/dL is considered protective |
| **Optimal range** | 50-80 mg/dL. Functional medicine (ODX): 55-85. Rupa Health optimal: 58-77. For an active male, HDL in the 50-70 range is excellent. Exercise reliably raises HDL. Very high HDL (>90) should be interpreted cautiously. |
| **High values indicate** | Vigorous regular exercise (most common cause), moderate alcohol consumption, genetic factors (CETP deficiency), some medications. Very high HDL (>100) may indicate dysfunctional HDL in some genetic contexts. |
| **Low values indicate** | Metabolic syndrome, insulin resistance, sedentary lifestyle, smoking, high refined-carb diet, type 2 diabetes, obesity, certain medications (beta-blockers, anabolic steroids), hypertriglyceridemia |
| **Special considerations** | HDL is the most exercise-responsive lipid marker. Aerobic exercise can raise HDL 5-10%. Moderate alcohol raises HDL. Smoking lowers HDL. The triglyceride/HDL ratio is a powerful insulin resistance proxy: ratio <2.0 is ideal, >3.0 suggests insulin resistance. |
| **Interactions with other markers** | Triglycerides (TG/HDL ratio), total cholesterol/HDL ratio, LDL, ApoB, fasting insulin, glucose, waist circumference. |

---

### 3.4 Triglycerides (TG)

| Field | Content |
|-------|---------|
| **What it measures** | Blood concentration of triglycerides, the most common form of fat in the body. Triglycerides are packaged in VLDL particles by the liver and in chylomicrons from dietary fat absorption. |
| **Why it matters** | Elevated triglycerides are a key feature of metabolic syndrome and insulin resistance. High triglycerides independently increase cardiovascular risk, promote small dense LDL formation, and at very high levels (>500) cause pancreatitis. |
| **Standard reference range** | <150 mg/dL normal; 150-199 borderline; 200-499 high; >=500 very high (Quest/Labcorp/ATP III) |
| **Optimal range** | <100 mg/dL, ideally <75 mg/dL. Functional medicine targets <100. Peter Attia and longevity practitioners aim for <100. The TG/HDL ratio is a powerful metabolic health indicator: <1.0 is excellent, <2.0 is good, >3.0 suggests insulin resistance. |
| **High values indicate** | Insulin resistance/metabolic syndrome, excessive carbohydrate intake (especially refined carbs and fructose), alcohol, obesity, diabetes, hypothyroidism, kidney disease, medications (beta-blockers, thiazides, estrogen, retinoids), familial hypertriglyceridemia |
| **Low values indicate** | Low fat diet, malnutrition, malabsorption, hyperthyroidism, certain genetic conditions. Very low TG (<40) is unusual but generally not harmful. |
| **Special considerations** | **Must be fasting (12-14 hours ideally) for accurate measurement.** Triglycerides are the most meal-responsive lipid marker. A non-fasting sample can show triglycerides 2-3x higher than fasting. Alcohol the night before significantly raises triglycerides. High-carb meals the night before raise triglycerides. |
| **Interactions with other markers** | HDL (TG/HDL ratio), VLDL (TG/5 = VLDL approximately), LDL calculation (affected by TG), fasting insulin, glucose, HbA1c, liver enzymes, uric acid. |

---

### 3.5 VLDL Cholesterol

| Field | Content |
|-------|---------|
| **What it measures** | Cholesterol carried in very-low-density lipoprotein particles. VLDL is produced by the liver and is the primary carrier of triglycerides in the fasting state. Usually estimated as triglycerides/5. |
| **Why it matters** | VLDL is an atherogenic lipoprotein. Elevated VLDL reflects excess triglyceride production by the liver, often driven by insulin resistance, excess carbohydrate intake, or alcohol. |
| **Standard reference range** | 5-40 mg/dL (Quest/Labcorp) |
| **Optimal range** | <20 mg/dL. Since VLDL is approximately triglycerides/5, optimal triglycerides <100 mg/dL corresponds to VLDL <20 mg/dL. |
| **High values indicate** | Insulin resistance, metabolic syndrome, high carbohydrate diet, obesity, alcohol, diabetes, familial dyslipidemia |
| **Low values indicate** | Low fat/carbohydrate diet, malnutrition, hyperthyroidism, liver disease |
| **Special considerations** | VLDL is almost always estimated (TG/5), not directly measured. The estimate becomes inaccurate when triglycerides >400 mg/dL. Fasting is required. VLDL particles are metabolized to LDL particles in the bloodstream, so high VLDL drives high LDL. |
| **Interactions with other markers** | Triglycerides (directly proportional), LDL, ApoB, fasting insulin, liver function tests. |

---

## 4. Hemoglobin A1c (HbA1c)

| Field | Content |
|-------|---------|
| **What it measures** | Percentage of hemoglobin molecules that are glycated (have glucose attached). Reflects average blood glucose levels over the preceding 2-3 months (the lifespan of a red blood cell). |
| **Why it matters** | The gold standard for assessing long-term glucose control. Unlike fasting glucose (a single snapshot), HbA1c captures cumulative glucose exposure. Peter Attia emphasizes that glucose control exists on a spectrum and that even within the "normal" range, lower is better for longevity. HbA1c of 4.6% corresponds to an average glucose of ~85 mg/dL; 5.6% corresponds to ~114 mg/dL -- both are "normal" but represent very different metabolic realities. |
| **Standard reference range** | <5.7% normal; 5.7-6.4% prediabetes; >=6.5% diabetes (ADA criteria). Lab range: 3.9-6.2% (NCBI). |
| **Optimal range** | 4.8-5.2%, ideally <5.0%. Peter Attia considers 4.6% (average glucose ~85) to be optimal and sees the climb from 4.6% to 5.6% as a meaningful metabolic deterioration even though both are "normal." Functional medicine: 5.0-5.4% (most sources). Mark Hyman: <5.3%. For aggressive longevity optimization: 4.6-5.2%. |
| **High values indicate** | Insulin resistance, prediabetes, type 2 diabetes, poor glucose control, metabolic syndrome. Even HbA1c of 5.5-5.6% (still "normal") is associated with meaningfully increased cardiovascular and all-cause mortality risk compared to 5.0%. |
| **Low values indicate** | Very low average blood glucose. HbA1c <4.0% may indicate: hemolytic anemia (shortened RBC lifespan), chronic blood loss, liver disease, or certain hemoglobin variants. Note: low-carb dieters may achieve legitimately low HbA1c (~4.5-4.8%) without pathology. |
| **Special considerations** | **Does not require fasting.** HbA1c is unreliable in conditions that alter RBC lifespan: hemolytic anemia, blood loss, iron deficiency anemia (falsely elevates HbA1c because RBCs live longer when iron-deficient), sickle cell disease, thalassemia. Hemoglobin variants (HbS, HbC) can interfere with some assays. Fructosamine is an alternative 2-3 week glucose average marker when HbA1c is unreliable. |
| **Interactions with other markers** | Fasting glucose, fasting insulin, HOMA-IR, C-peptide, triglycerides, triglyceride/HDL ratio, CGM data (if available). |

---

## 5. Fasting Insulin

| Field | Content |
|-------|---------|
| **What it measures** | Serum concentration of insulin after an overnight fast of 8-12 hours. Insulin is the hormone produced by pancreatic beta cells that enables glucose uptake by cells and regulates blood sugar. |
| **Why it matters** | **The single most important early marker of metabolic dysfunction that most standard panels omit.** Fasting insulin rises years before fasting glucose or HbA1c become abnormal. It is the earliest detectable sign of insulin resistance. By the time fasting glucose is elevated, insulin resistance has typically been present for 5-15 years. Elevated fasting insulin is linked to obesity, cardiovascular disease, cancer risk, Alzheimer's disease, and accelerated aging. |
| **Standard reference range** | 2.6-24.9 uIU/mL (Quest); <25 uIU/mL (Labcorp). Note how absurdly wide the "normal" range is -- a fasting insulin of 20 is technically "normal" but represents significant insulin resistance. |
| **Optimal range** | 2-5 uIU/mL. Mark Hyman: 2-5 uIU/mL. Root Functional Medicine: 2-6 uIU/mL. Multiple functional medicine sources converge on <5 as ideal. Peter Attia does not publish a specific number but emphasizes that fasting insulin should be as low as possible while maintaining normal glucose. HOMA-IR (fasting insulin x fasting glucose / 405) should be <1.0 optimally (<2.0 at minimum). |
| **High values indicate** | Insulin resistance (the primary concern), metabolic syndrome, early type 2 diabetes (body compensating by producing more insulin), PCOS, insulinoma (rare), Cushing's syndrome, acromegaly, obesity, excessive carbohydrate intake |
| **Low values indicate** | Type 1 diabetes (beta cell destruction), late-stage type 2 diabetes (beta cell burnout), hypopituitarism, adrenal insufficiency. Very low fasting insulin (<2) in the presence of normal glucose is generally ideal and reflects excellent insulin sensitivity. |
| **Special considerations** | **Must be fasting 10-14 hours.** More sensitive to fasting duration than glucose. Exercise within 24-48 hours can lower fasting insulin (acute effect). Chronic exercise improves insulin sensitivity long-term. Stress and cortisol raise insulin. Time of day matters. It can take 12+ months of dietary/lifestyle intervention to meaningfully lower fasting insulin. Calculate HOMA-IR alongside: (fasting insulin x fasting glucose) / 405. |
| **Interactions with other markers** | Fasting glucose (HOMA-IR calculation), HbA1c, C-peptide, triglycerides, HDL, triglyceride/HDL ratio (>3.0 = insulin resistance), waist circumference, liver enzymes (NAFLD link), uric acid. |

---

## 6. Fasting C-Peptide

| Field | Content |
|-------|---------|
| **What it measures** | Serum concentration of C-peptide after an overnight fast. C-peptide is a 31-amino-acid byproduct of insulin production -- when proinsulin is cleaved to form insulin, C-peptide is released in equimolar (1:1) amounts. |
| **Why it matters** | C-peptide is a more reliable measure of endogenous insulin production than insulin itself because: (1) it has a longer half-life (30-35 minutes vs. 3-5 minutes for insulin), (2) it is not extracted by the liver on first pass (insulin is ~50% cleared), and (3) it is not affected by exogenous insulin administration. Critical for distinguishing type 1 vs. type 2 diabetes, detecting insulinoma, and assessing beta cell function. |
| **Standard reference range** | 0.8-3.85 ng/mL (Quest); 1.1-4.4 ng/mL (Labcorp); 0.78-1.89 ng/mL fasting (Medscape) |
| **Optimal range** | 0.8-1.5 ng/mL fasting. Functional medicine targets the lower end of the reference range, reflecting adequate but not excessive insulin production (i.e., good insulin sensitivity). Values >2.0 ng/mL fasting suggest the pancreas is working harder than ideal to maintain glucose -- early insulin resistance signal. |
| **High values indicate** | Insulin resistance (pancreas compensating with excess insulin production), type 2 diabetes (early stage), insulinoma, kidney disease (C-peptide is renally cleared, so kidney impairment raises levels), Cushing's syndrome |
| **Low values indicate** | Type 1 diabetes (beta cell destruction), late-stage type 2 diabetes (beta cell exhaustion), exogenous insulin use (C-peptide is suppressed by exogenous insulin), pancreatectomy |
| **Special considerations** | **Must be fasting.** C-peptide is degraded by the kidneys -- interpret cautiously if eGFR is reduced. More stable than insulin for assessing beta cell function because of longer half-life and lack of hepatic extraction. Not affected by exogenous insulin or insulin antibodies. |
| **Interactions with other markers** | Fasting insulin (should correlate), fasting glucose, HbA1c, eGFR (renal clearance of C-peptide), GAD antibodies (type 1 diabetes autoimmunity). |

## 7. GGT (Gamma-Glutamyl Transferase)

| Field | Content |
|-------|---------|
| **What it measures** | Serum level of gamma-glutamyl transferase, an enzyme found primarily in the liver (also kidneys, pancreas, spleen). GGT catalyzes the transfer of gamma-glutamyl groups and plays a central role in glutathione metabolism. |
| **Why it matters** | Far more than a liver enzyme -- GGT is emerging as one of the most powerful predictors of cardiometabolic disease, all-cause mortality, and metabolic dysfunction. Elevated GGT signals that the body is depleting glutathione (its master antioxidant) faster than it can replenish it, reflecting oxidative stress. GGT is associated with: cardiovascular disease, type 2 diabetes, metabolic syndrome, fatty liver disease, cancer, and all-cause mortality -- even within the "normal" range. |
| **Standard reference range** | 0-65 IU/L males (Labcorp); 3-70 U/L males 20-29, up to 3-95 males 40-54 (Quest, age-stratified); 0-60 IU/L females (Labcorp) |
| **Optimal range** | <25 U/L for males, ideally 10-20 U/L. ODX functional optimal: 10-17 U/L. Functional medicine (MindBody): 10-29 males. Longevity health experts: <25 for men. The standard "normal" range (up to 65-95) is far too permissive -- research shows increasing cardiovascular and all-cause mortality risk at GGT levels well within the standard range. |
| **High values indicate** | Alcohol use (GGT is the most sensitive enzyme for alcohol), fatty liver disease (NAFLD/MASLD), biliary obstruction, medications (many drugs are metabolized via glutathione pathways), obesity, metabolic syndrome, oxidative stress, pancreatitis, diabetes, heart failure, environmental toxin exposure |
| **Low values indicate** | Generally not clinically concerning. Very low GGT may reflect hypothyroidism or familial intrahepatic cholestasis (rare). |
| **Special considerations** | GGT is the most alcohol-sensitive liver enzyme and is often used to monitor alcohol abstinence (normalizes within 2-4 weeks of cessation). Even moderate alcohol consumption can elevate GGT. Medications that induce liver enzymes (anticonvulsants, barbiturates) raise GGT. Obesity and metabolic syndrome raise GGT independent of alcohol. When ALP is elevated, GGT helps determine if the source is liver (GGT also elevated) vs. bone (GGT normal). |
| **Interactions with other markers** | ALP (liver vs. bone source), ALT, AST, bilirubin, fasting insulin (metabolic syndrome), triglycerides, uric acid, hs-CRP, ferritin (iron overload can raise GGT). |

---

## 8. Uric Acid

| Field | Content |
|-------|---------|
| **What it measures** | Serum concentration of uric acid, the end product of purine metabolism (purines come from dietary sources and cellular turnover of DNA/RNA). Uric acid is produced primarily in the liver and excreted by the kidneys (~70%) and gut (~30%). |
| **Why it matters** | Traditionally associated only with gout, uric acid is now recognized as a significant cardiovascular and metabolic risk factor. Elevated uric acid is strongly associated with: hypertension, cardiovascular disease, chronic kidney disease, metabolic syndrome, type 2 diabetes, fatty liver disease, and all-cause mortality. Dr. David Perlmutter (Drop Acid) has popularized the metabolic dangers of elevated uric acid. Uric acid has a U-shaped relationship with health: both very high and very low levels are associated with increased risk. |
| **Standard reference range** | 2.5-7.0 mg/dL males (Labcorp); 4.0-8.0 mg/dL (ACCP); 3.5-7.2 mg/dL (Quest) |
| **Optimal range** | <5.5 mg/dL, ideally 3.5-5.0 mg/dL. David Perlmutter: <5.5 mg/dL. Japanese cardiovascular study (PMC): <5.0 for men for lowest cardiometabolic disease risk. Functional medicine (ODX): 3.2-5.5. Multiple longevity sources converge on <5.5, with <5.0 being ideal. The standard upper limit of 7.0-8.0 is set to detect gout, not cardiometabolic risk. |
| **High values indicate** | Gout, high-purine diet (organ meats, shellfish, sardines, anchovies), excessive fructose intake (fructose uniquely raises uric acid via ATP depletion), alcohol (especially beer), kidney disease (reduced excretion), metabolic syndrome, obesity, dehydration, myeloproliferative disorders (increased cell turnover), medications (diuretics, low-dose aspirin, cyclosporine), tumor lysis syndrome |
| **Low values indicate** | Low purine diet, Fanconi syndrome, Wilson's disease, SIADH, xanthine oxidase deficiency. Very low uric acid (<2.0) may impair antioxidant defense (uric acid accounts for ~50% of plasma antioxidant capacity). |
| **Special considerations** | Fructose (from sugar-sweetened beverages, high-fructose corn syrup, agave, excessive fruit juice) is a primary dietary driver of elevated uric acid -- it uniquely depletes cellular ATP during metabolism, generating purines as a byproduct. Alcohol (especially beer, which contains purines) raises uric acid both by increasing production and decreasing excretion. Dehydration concentrates uric acid. Intense exercise transiently raises uric acid. Low-dose aspirin (<1g/day) reduces renal excretion of uric acid and can raise levels. |
| **Interactions with other markers** | Kidney function (creatinine, eGFR -- uric acid is renally cleared), triglycerides, fasting insulin, glucose, blood pressure, GGT, ALT (metabolic syndrome cluster), hs-CRP. |

## Key Calculated Ratios

| Ratio | Formula | Optimal | Significance |
|-------|---------|---------|-------------|
| **HOMA-IR** | (Fasting Insulin x Fasting Glucose) / 405 | <1.0 | Insulin resistance index. <1.0 excellent, <2.0 acceptable, >2.0 insulin resistant |
| **TG/HDL Ratio** | Triglycerides / HDL | <1.0 ideal; <2.0 good | Insulin resistance proxy. >3.0 = likely insulin resistant |
| **Total Chol/HDL Ratio** | Total Cholesterol / HDL | <3.5 | Cardiovascular risk. <3.5 ideal, >5.0 elevated risk |
| **AST/ALT (De Ritis) Ratio** | AST / ALT | ~1.0 | >2.0 suggests alcoholic liver disease; <1.0 with both elevated suggests NAFLD |
| **BUN/Creatinine Ratio** | BUN / Creatinine | 10:1 to 20:1 | >20:1 suggests pre-renal cause (dehydration, GI bleed) |
| **Anion Gap** | Na - (Cl + CO2) | 8-12 mEq/L | Elevated in metabolic acidosis (DKA, lactic acidosis, toxins) |
| **Non-HDL Cholesterol** | Total Cholesterol - HDL | <130 (standard); <100 (optimal) | Captures all atherogenic particles; better than LDL-C alone |
